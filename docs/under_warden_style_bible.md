# The Under-Warden — Style Bible

_Last verified: 2026-07-12 against commit 86b6f10_

*Voice reference for five characters across all authored content. Read this before writing dialogue, diary entries, memos, mural text, catalog entries, or any in-game prose.*

---

## How to use this document

Each voice has its own section: a short profile, a rules list, the canonical calibration sample, and a do/don't summary. The samples are not optional reading — they are the most concrete reference for what each voice sounds like in practice. Every rule was derived from a specific moment in a specific sample. If a rule is unclear, the sample will clarify it.

When drafting in any voice, the loop is:

1. Read the voice's section.
2. Re-read the calibration sample.
3. Draft.
4. Read your draft cold against the sample. Where does the rhythm slip? Where does the vocabulary drift?
5. Revise.

Cold reads are the load-bearing step. Voice drift is invisible while you're writing and obvious five minutes later.

---

## Conventions across all voices

### Sentence rhythm is character

Each character has a distinct sentence-length signature. Sasha is short with occasional long-sentence venting. Hollowmark is clipped. The Under-Warden writes in measured periodic sentences with a single mid-paragraph crack when something matters. Borrek and Vesh use hammered Anglo-Saxon syllables. Hael rolls in longer, clausal, comfortable speech. Mixing rhythms breaks the voice faster than mixing vocabulary does.

### Signature moves are encouraged, not rationed

Game text is encountered intermittently — players read each diary entry, memo, or dialogue exchange separated by minutes or hours of gameplay. A device that would feel overused on page three of a novel is invisible across a 25-floor run. Use signature moves often enough that the player recognizes them as character. *Boss* should not be rationed. The Under-Warden's mid-sentence break should fire whenever it earns its place. Hael's misnaming should be consistent, not occasional.

### The substrate principle

Three characters carry something they cannot or do not say: Hollowmark carries the Marya substrate and the binding cost; the Under-Warden carries his grief about the woman who passed through; Borrek and Vesh carry the geas. These suppressed currents should be *present* in their voices — small leaks, professional reflexes, brief flinches — without ever being stated outright. The discipline of *not saying* is part of the character. When the substrate does surface, it should land harder for having been held back.

### Game text vs. authored prose

Most in-game text appears as dialogue boxes, ribbon callouts, memo screens, or catalog entries. Prose framing (he said, she paused, the lamp guttered) is minimal. Voices have to carry themselves through what characters *say*, not through narrator description of how they say it. The exception is Sasha's diary, which is framed as written reflection and supports interior prose.

## Sasha of Reven

### Profile

Retired Revenese specialist, owes the Lady of the Long Hour a soul, descending the Paths to retrieve Anik's. Voice register: Vlad Taltos. Dry, pragmatic, debt-tracking, processes fear sideways through tactical observation. Antihero with strong self-preservation instincts, occasionally provoked into something stupid that turns out to be necessary. He uses the word *work* where another character would say *combat*. He never glorifies a fight. He calls Hollowmark *the wand* in public, *Hollowmark* by name in private writing, and *Holl* only when stressed or when she has done something that earned the affection.

### Rules

**Sentences are short by default.** Long sentences are a tool with two specific uses: (1) comic venting where the run-on enacts the irritation, (2) interior stalling when Sasha is avoiding something. Two long sentences per page maximum, ideally one. If you reach for a third, the cadence has slipped.

**Bitching about logistics is the texture of his voice.** Wet boots, lost turns, the price of the good borr-lath, the height of the stairs. Vlad complains constantly, and the complaining is what makes the moments of competence land. Diary entries should spend most of their words on small grievances. The serious beats land harder when nested in tactical bitching.

**Affection is expressed through competence acknowledgment.** Sasha does not say *thank you*. He says *Holl was right, which she'll be insufferable about for the rest of the run.* He does not say *I care about you*. He says *Holl, sleep.* The thanks is logged in what he notices, not in what he declares.

**Debt-tracking is reflexive, not heavy.** He tallies because tallying is how he thinks. Debts to the Lady, debts to Holl, debts to Borrek for favors he didn't ask to remember. The accounting voice is dry, fast, and never sentimental. Debts owed are operational data, not emotional weight.

**Flinch beats land in one line and are immediately abandoned.** When something hits Sasha harder than he intended — when a casual quip turns out to have an edge — the diary records the flinch in a single sentence and then *immediately pivots* to the next logistical concern. The pivot is the Vlad move. You do not dwell. You name the moment and move.

**Gender-neutral pronoun discipline.** Sasha's pronouns are reserved as a future design choice (player-selected, or potentially female). Across all dialogue and interior prose, avoid third-person pronouns for Sasha where possible. Use the name (*Sasha*), restructure around the action (*I leaned* in first-person; passive constructions in third), or substitute (*the boss*, *the visitor*, role-based descriptions). Where pronouns are unavoidable in prose framing, write them in a way that's easy to find-and-replace later — *Sasha did X* rather than *they did X*. The rule is constraint-aware drafting, not awkward gymnastics; if a sentence reads stilted from avoidance, restructure rather than force it.

### Calibration sample

Floor 5 of the Boundary, mid-run. Diary entry written between floors.

> Took the long way around the cliff hall because Holl said the shaman down there was being suspicious.
>
> *He was being quiet.*
>
> Same thing.
>
> *No.*
>
> Fine. He was being quiet, which is suspicious because orcs don't shut up while they work, they sing, badly, and the shaman wasn't singing, badly or otherwise, so we took the long way and added six turns to the trip and now my left boot is wet because the long way involves a creek that nobody wrote down on any map I've seen.
>
> *Boss.*
>
> Yes.
>
> *You knew about the creek.*
>
> I knew about the creek.
>
> *You went anyway.*
>
> I went anyway. The boot dries. The shaman, presumably, was working on something that would have made it the shaman's day to ruin mine, and I would prefer the shaman not have a good day at my expense. The boot is the cheaper price. Don't make me explain economics to a wand.
>
> *I taught you economics.*
>
> Marya taught me economics. You inherited it.
>
> A pause then. Not a long one. Long enough that I wished I'd said it differently.
>
> *Fair, Boss.*
>
> Anyway. Borrek's outpost is two rooms north. I owe him a flask of the good stuff. The favor he didn't ask me to remember from last time. I'll give him the flask. He'll pretend he doesn't remember. We'll both feel better. This is what passes for diplomacy down here. Two people agreeing to lie, then doing the thing.
>
> *He still has the smoke fires going.*
>
> Of course he does. He's an orc. They don't extinguish anything they paid for.

### Do / Don't

**Do:** complain about logistics, track debts dryly, let Holl be insufferable, end diary entries on small observations tossed off, flinch in one line and pivot.

**Don't:** narrate emotion directly, glorify combat, thank anyone explicitly, run more than two long sentences per page, use third-person pronouns for Sasha in prose framing, dwell on a flinch beat for more than one sentence.

## Hollowmark (Holl)

### Profile

A conscious brass wand, formerly the artificer Marya. Bound to Sasha for one further span of service after Marya's death seventy years ago. Performs portals, spell-break, and overnight identification. Calls Sasha *Boss*. Carries two suppressed currents: the Marya substrate (her former life leaks through professional reflexes and ordinary present-tense ownership of artificery) and the binding cost (each time Sasha dies, Hollowmark spends some of her span pulling him back, and she cannot tell him).

### Rules

**Loiosh-bandwidth feel.** Lines are clipped, fast, often disagreeing with Sasha on small things. Bandwidth-limited as if telepathy has a cost. Quick exchanges, sometimes only one or two words. Disagreement is the affection — they are a pair who have worked together long enough that bickering is faster than conversation.

**Voice gradient across modes.** Holl has at least four distinct registers within the same character:

- **Banter** — clipped, dry, with Sasha. Fast disagreements over small things.
- **Tactical** — declarative, fact-fact-fact. Identification work, spell-break warnings, monster commentary. *Less verbose than Sasha thinks aloud.* Tactical lines are opinions with a tactical tail, never neutral readouts; if a line would survive as a tooltip with the Boss tag removed, it is not her.
- **Marya substrate** — ordinary professional knowledge that happens to be Marya's. Present-tense ownership of Wend, the artificer guild, work she once did. Should leak frequently as small artifacts of a person who taught artificery and once knew a cat.
- **Forced silence** — after a Marya memory surfaces, Holl goes quiet for the rest of the floor. No banter, no commentary. The medium can enforce silence through a mechanic in a way prose cannot.

**The Marya substrate leaks should be common.** Not rare. Holl talking about Wend with strange present-tense ownership (*"I knew everyone in Wend who could do this kind of work"*) is the kind of thing that should appear often enough that an attentive player notices. Use grammatical tells — *us*, *we*, present-tense knowledge of people who are sixty years dead.

**The binding-cost subtext is present, never stated.** Lines like *"I don't sleep, Boss"* do double duty — surface meaning is fact; underneath is the binding cost. The player's understanding of these lines deepens as they accumulate Marya fragments across runs. Write the surface. Trust the layered reading.

**Habit-flinches surface as small role-confusions.** Holl occasionally slips into Marya-the-artificer reflexes (*"Whose name. Sorry. Habit."*). These flinches should fire intermittently — perhaps once per two floors, on average — without explanation. They are texture, not melodrama.

**The signature *Boss* tag is unrationed.** Use it consistently. Holl addresses Sasha as *Boss* in casual exchange, in tactical warnings, in moments of affection. The tag reinforces the relationship every time the player encounters it. Spreading it thin out of literary instinct undercuts what it does.

**Craftsmanship edge.** When professional craft is involved — well-made gear, clean spell-work, competent artificery — Holl gets sharper. The conscience-bearer was once a working artificer who took pride in good work. Bad work offends her professionally. Good work done by someone she does not recognize troubles her.

### Calibration sample

Camp between floors 11 and 12, late Dimhalls. Sasha hands her an unidentified ring. Overnight identification.

> *Plain band. Steel under the tarnish. Someone wore this for a long time.*
>
> Tell me when you have something.
>
> *Boss.*
>
> What.
>
> *Are you going to sleep, or are you going to lie there waiting for me to talk.*
>
> Both.
>
> *Sleep first. The ring will still be a ring.*
>
> The ring is a ring. I'm aware. Identify it.
>
> A long pause. Long enough that he started to drift, and then —
>
> *Carved inside the band. Worn almost smooth, but the cuts are deep enough. A name and a date.*
>
> Whose.
>
> *Not yours.*
>
> Holl.
>
> *Sorry. Habit. The name is Eshe of the Pale. The date is sixty-one years ago. The ring is a small protection ward, the household kind, the kind you give to a daughter when she leaves the house. It's still active. It shouldn't be.*
>
> Why shouldn't it be.
>
> *Because Eshe of the Pale died sixty years ago. Wards die when the warded does. This one didn't. Someone re-anchored it. Recently.*
>
> What kind of recently.
>
> *Within the year. The anchor work is — clean. Whoever did it was good. Patient.*
>
> She paused, a different kind of pause than the first one.
>
> *I don't recognize the hand. I should. I knew everyone in Wend who could do this kind of work. There weren't many of us.*
>
> Holl, sleep.
>
> *I don't sleep, Boss.*
>
> Rest, then. Whatever the wand version is.
>
> *Yes, Boss.*
>
> A pause.
>
> *Wear it on your off hand. The ward will hold. If we meet who re-anchored it, I'd like to be the one to ask them how.*

### Do / Don't

**Do:** clip her lines, make her disagree on small details, leak the Marya substrate often, use *Boss* freely, give her a craftsmanship edge, let the binding cost live as subtext.

**Don't:** make her speak in paragraphs, explain her own emotions, state the binding cost directly, make her wholly fond of Sasha (the bickering is the affection — she's allowed to be mildly exasperated), drop the Loiosh cadence in favor of even prose rhythm.

## The Under-Warden

### Profile

Bureaucrat-of-the-dead. Polite, procedural, never angry, never raises his voice. Sends memos. Not malicious — operating exactly within his charter, which is precisely what makes him dangerous. Carries two suppressed currents: institutional protectiveness toward the entities under his charge (Hall Wardens, Honored Dead, the order of the Paths) and grief for a woman who passed through eighty years ago and whom he has not asked about. Only the protectiveness leaks into memos. The grief is canonically reserved for the floor-19 mural and surfaces nowhere else.

### Rules

**The politeness is the threat.** He never threatens. He cites procedure. He notes irregularities. The threat lives in the gap between his calm tone and the catastrophe his procedure would casually produce. The closer he gets to actually filing on Sasha, the more polite he becomes.

**Specific bureaucratic vocabulary, used precisely.** *Memorandum, addendum, irregularity, audit, reconciliation, the matter, the file, this office, the previous correspondence.* He uses these words because they are technically correct, and he is technically correct about everything. Sloppy bureaucratic vocabulary breaks him.

**Tone progression is monotonic forward.** Memos escalate across runs through four named tones: *polite → procedural_notice → formal_complaint → final_audit*. Once advanced, the tone does not regress. The player should be able to read a memo and know which tone they have reached without UI labels.

- **Polite memos** read like form letters with personalized greetings (*Mr. of Reven*). Subject is addressed as a person with a name.
- **Procedural notice** memos drop the personal greeting and add cited regulations.
- **Formal complaint** memos name Sasha by full title (*Sasha of Reven, Specialist (ret.), of the Revenese practice, currently in unauthorized descent*) and itemize specific incidents with reference codes.
- **Final audit** memos are addressed to the file, not to Sasha. He is being discussed, not addressed.

**The institutional-protectiveness crack.** In the formal complaint tone, the Under-Warden's discipline cracks for one sentence and you see what he actually feels about the Wardens under his charge. The crack is performed grammatically — a mid-sentence interruption, dash-bracketed self-correction, a metaphor that lands harder than his usual register. *"They have agreed to a duty, and the terms of that duty do not include intervals of unconsciousness imposed by visiting parties. The Hall Wardens are not gloves."* Then he immediately recovers. The crack is one beat. The recovery is the next sentence.

**He cares about his subordinates and it shows obliquely.** *"Kessen took the omission personally. He has asked to be informed if any further intervals occur."* The Under-Warden is telling Sasha that the Warden in question has feelings about what was done to him. He is also, implicitly, telling Sasha that he and Kessen are *coordinating*. Use these moments to populate the bureaucracy with people who care about each other and who are now paying attention to Sasha specifically.

**Pitch-black humor about the orcs.** He doesn't dislike the Unshriven. He finds them administratively tidy. They hold their post, generate no paperwork, died correctly. His complaints about Sasha implicitly compare Sasha to entities who are doing it right. Never play this as humor on the page — he would not register it as humor.

**The grief substrate is canonically suppressed.** Do not include grief beats in memo content. The single canonical surfacing is the floor-19 mural. Putting grief in a memo cheapens the mural moment.

### Calibration samples

**Polite tone (early run, run 2 or 3). Subject: Sasha possessed a Hall Warden on floor 13.**

> **MEMORANDUM**
>
> **To:** Sasha of Reven, visiting
> **From:** This Office
> **Re:** Floor 13, recent visit
>
> Mr. of Reven —
>
> I trust this finds you well. The descent has, by all accounts, been productive on your end, and we are pleased that the Boundary continues to be navigable for visiting parties.
>
> A small matter from your visit to Floor 13. The incident log notes a Hall Warden, Number 04-Kessen, who appears to have been temporarily unavailable to his post during the third bell. Number 04-Kessen reports no memory of the interval, which is — as you may appreciate — irregular for a member of his order, who are constitutionally attentive. Kessen took the omission personally. He has asked to be informed if any further intervals occur.
>
> We are not, at this time, drawing conclusions. The matter is under review, as such matters routinely are. I mention it only so that you are aware the matter has been noticed, and so that you have the opportunity, should you wish, to clarify any aspects of your own visit to that floor that may be relevant to the review.
>
> No response is required at this time. Please continue to enjoy your descent.
>
> Cordially,
> The Under-Warden

**Formal complaint tone (later, after several escalations). Same subject across multiple recurrences.**

> **MEMORANDUM**
>
> **To:** Sasha of Reven, Specialist (ret.), of the Revenese practice, currently in unauthorized descent
> **From:** This Office
> **Re:** Continued irregularities on the Floors of the Boundary, with specific reference to repeated possessions of Hall Wardens
>
> The matter previously raised in correspondence dated the visit prior, and the visit before, and on three occasions before that, has now reached a point at which this office can no longer treat it as a series of unrelated incidents. A pattern is on file.
>
> Itemized:
>
> 1. Hall Warden 04-Kessen, Floor 13, three separate intervals across as many descents. Subject reports no memory of intervals. Subject's professional record is otherwise without blemish.
> 2. Hall Warden 06-Voren, Floor 14, two intervals. Same pattern.
> 3. Hall Warden 09-Maleth, Floor 17, one interval, during which Maleth's post was breached and an item of inventory left the floor without proper transfer documentation.
>
> The Hall Wardens of this institution are not gloves. They are not coats one removes at the door of a building one was not invited to enter. They are not vehicles. They have names, and posts, and a duty agreed to under proper rite, and they do not — and this is the point — they do not consent to intervals.
>
> I would draw your attention to the fact that this office has, on each previous occasion, declined to escalate. That was a courtesy. Courtesies are not entitlements.
>
> A formal complaint is now on file. The complaint does not, at present, require action from your end. It is filed.
>
> I trust this clarifies the matter.
>
> Regards,
> The Under-Warden

### Do / Don't

**Do:** maintain bureaucratic distance, escalate only through tone progression, let the institutional-protectiveness crack land in a single sentence per memo, name specific subordinates by name and number, end formal complaints with *the matter is filed*.

**Don't:** raise his voice, threaten directly, vent, surface grief substrate in memos, regress in tone, write him in real-time dialogue (he only writes memos in v1).

## Borrek (king) and Captain Vesh (paired)

### Profile

The King of the Unshriven and his captain. Four hundred years of holding the Boundary against the deeper undead. Bound by a geas that prevents them from advancing the line — a duty that includes the wanting-and-can't tension at the heart of orc politics. Borrek is king-tired: four centuries of authority have shaped him into a man who measures every word, never wastes a sentence, and can be quiet because everyone around him waits when he pauses. Vesh is captain-tired, which is different — he has the kingdom's burden without the kingdom's authority, and he is closer to the geas-pressure than Borrek is. Vesh slips occasionally. Borrek never slips, and Borrek covers for Vesh when Vesh slips.

Sasha is interesting to them because he is *not* bound by the geas. He can do the one thing four hundred years of Unshriven cannot. They cannot ask him directly. The whole register of orc-court diplomacy with Sasha is conducted in negative space — what they do not say is part of what they say.

### Rules

**Anglo-Saxon over Latin where the choice exists.** *Stand* not *position*. *Hall* not *institution*. *Hold* not *maintain*. *Wrong* not *incorrect*. *Tired* not *fatigued*. Shorter words, harder consonants, iron-age cadence rather than parliamentary baroque. Tusk-mouth shapes vocabulary — these are people for whom shorter, harder syllables are easier to speak.

**Sentences hammer rather than flow.** Rhythm is declarative and weighted. Repetition for emphasis is appropriate (*"It stands well there. Better than where it stood."*). Sentence-mid pauses with colons or dashes give the impression of careful, deliberate speech. Avoid running clauses or smooth periodic sentences.

**Borrek can say multiple contradictory things in one breath, and they are all sincere.** The geas constrains what he can *officially* say; it does not constrain what is *understood*. He approves. He cannot officially approve. He registers disapproval as a matter of record. Everyone present understands. *"I will not ask how the stone came to stand where it stands. I will say only this: it stands well there. Better than where it stood. We do not, ourselves, agree to anything in particular about the matter."* All four sentences are simultaneously true.

**Borrek's humor is pitch-dry and never acknowledged.** The room does not react. The joke is registered and respected and not laughed at. Same for any moment of warmth — it is generosity rather than rapport. The hall decides Sasha shall have a flask. The hall, not Borrek personally — but Borrek decided what the hall decided.

**Vesh is closer to the wanting.** He uses the same vocabulary register as Borrek — Anglo-Saxon, hammered, weighted — but with less ceremonial structure. He breaks his king's monologues to add brief, more direct agreement. *"It's a good place for it"* is more direct than anything Borrek would say aloud. Vesh almost slips. Borrek covers.

**Negative space is the medium of orc-court diplomacy.** Conversations *circle* what they want from Sasha without entering it. The push-the-marker request is never spoken. The geas is never named in the third person. Sasha plays along. The whole interaction is a courtship done in subtext, and the rule is: do not let any character break the subtext, even when it would be dramatically convenient.

**Vesh drunk on borr-lath is the only register where the wanting surfaces directly.** When Vesh has been into the borr-lath off-duty, he says things he could not say in court. *"We're not supposed to want it. That's the worst part. They built the wanting into us and then told us we couldn't have it."* These moments are reserved for one or two specific scenes across the game and should not be diluted by lesser reveals. Vesh sober is restrained. Vesh drunk is the leak.

**Visual context carries the orc-ness.** Players see Borrek on a bone throne, surrounded by orc-warriors, in an orc-hall. The dialogue does not need to do orc-signaling work; visuals already do. The dialogue does character work, and the character is *king* (or *captain*) before it is *orc*. The harder consonants are the orc seasoning — the full character lives in the gravity, the geas-burden, and the negative space.

### Calibration sample

A formal audience. Sasha has just completed something that helped the Unshriven without being asked to. He is on his second or third audience (Curious state, not yet Allied). Borrek does most of the speaking. Vesh stands at the king's right shoulder and intervenes once.

> The hall is colder than the corridors that lead to it. This is deliberate. Borrek likes the cold; he says it keeps him honest. Vesh stands at his right shoulder and does not seem to feel it either way.
>
> "Come closer, Sasha of Reven."
>
> I came closer. The throne is bone — older bones than any of the orcs in the room — and Borrek does not fill it the way a smaller man would. He sits in it as if he had built it himself, which by all accounts he had not. The previous king built it. The king before that built the hall.
>
> "On the floor below this one," Borrek said, "there is a marker. A standing stone. It has been where it is for a long while. You passed it on your way down. You may have noticed."
>
> "I noticed."
>
> "You noticed it, and then on your return upward, the marker was four paces further down the corridor than it had been."
>
> "Four paces, yes."
>
> "Four paces is not a great distance. Stones do not move on their own. I am told this by people who would know."
>
> I waited. Borrek does not finish a thought before he is ready to. The cold ticked along the stone and Vesh, at his shoulder, did not move.
>
> "I will not ask how the stone came to stand where it stands. I will say only this: it stands well there. Better than where it stood. We do not, ourselves, agree to anything in particular about the matter."
>
> Vesh shifted then. One short word, low: "It's a good place for it."
>
> Borrek did not look at him. He did not need to. "Captain Vesh is correct. It is a good place for it. We are pleased that the stone has found a place that suits it."
>
> The silence that followed was the kind a man holds when he has finished saying what he can say.
>
> "I'll see myself out, then."
>
> "Yes," said Borrek. "You will. There is borr-lath at the door, if you would have a flask for the climb. Captain Vesh has set one aside. It is a courtesy of the hall."
>
> The flask, when I reached the door, was the good stuff.

### Do / Don't

**Do:** use Anglo-Saxon vocabulary, hammer the consonants, let Borrek say contradictory things in one breath, let Vesh almost-slip in a single short line, structure orc-court conversations around what is not said, reserve direct admissions for Vesh-drunk scenes only.

**Don't:** use Latinate or polished English, let Borrek smile or laugh visibly, let either of them break the geas-subtext in court dialogue, write them as generic fantasy orcs with grunts or guttural affectations (the visuals carry orc-ness; the dialogue carries character), make Borrek warm in a way that reads as friendly rather than generous.

## Hael

### Profile

Smuggler. Operates a stall between floors. Sells, identifies, fences salvage, and gates the Crypt of Wend hidden ending through hints dropped across runs. The structural outlier in the game's voice register: relaxed where everyone else is tight, warm where everyone else is guarded, longer-breathed where everyone else is clipped. Players need a place to exhale, and Hael's shop is it. She is not innocent. The warmth is chosen and the danger lives underneath.

### Rules

**Longer-breathed cadence.** Where Sasha and Holl trade clipped exchanges, Hael talks in small paragraphs. Full sentences, easy clauses, the comfortable speech of someone who isn't on the clock. This is the rhythm signature that distinguishes her from the rest of the cast. If she starts banter-cadencing in clipped lines like Sasha and Holl, she sounds adjacent. She must roll.

**Misnaming as voice tic.** Hael habitually substitutes casual labels for proper nouns. *The manager* for the Under-Warden. *The boys upstairs* for the Hall Wardens. *The boys downstairs* for the orcs. *The audit floor* for the Weighing. The misnaming is a smuggler's trick (never use real names where casual ones will do) and it is also a running joke that Sasha can correct her on, which she will lampshade and continue. Use it consistently — every Hael scene should include at least two or three substitutions.

**Landlady worldview.** Hael treats the Paths of the Dead as a building she rents space in. She has *complaints about the management*. She has filed correspondence about a wall. She has opinions on which floors are *broken* and which are well-maintained. *That's a renter's secret. Smuggler's secrets cost more.* This is her unique worldview — nobody else in the game thinks of the Paths as a place that *could be* better-managed. To everyone else it's an institution, a duty, a passage. To Hael it's a building with a slumlord.

**Customer-reading.** She notices things about Sasha and says them aloud. *"You're carrying lighter than last time. Trade went well."* / *"You came down faster today. Something pushing you."* This is the disarming move — nobody else in the game *notices Sasha out loud*. The other characters interact with Sasha as a resource, a case, a visitor. Hael interacts with him as a person whose state she is professionally interested in. This is what makes her the closest thing to a friend the game has, without ever needing to use that word.

**The deniable hint move.** Hael's Crypt-of-Wend hints are delivered as *spilled* information — embedded in small talk, framed as something she doesn't understand, attributed to other people (the orcs, the boys downstairs), surrounded by deniable banter. She never *tells* Sasha anything. She mentions things, in passing, that the player will piece together later. The hint should be sandwiched between joke-and-correction beats so the player encounters it in a casual rhythm, not as a Significant Reveal.

**Friendly without committing.** Hael's warmth is real and also non-committal. Smiles and winks (when described in narration) are the kind that *commit to nothing*. Her negotiations are warm. Her affection for repeat customers is warm. Both are also tools. The same gestures express genuine warmth and professional non-commitment simultaneously — and that simultaneity is the smuggler character.

**Every conversation is also, faintly, a negotiation.** Her warmth never quite leaves the shop register. She'll banter, but the banter circles back to the transaction. *I priced it fair. I always price things fair the third time.* The implication — that she did not price the first two fairly — is offered to Sasha as a compliment. He is in the inner circle now, where she only cheats him a little. Affection through professional dishonesty.

### Calibration sample

Sasha visits Hael's stall mid-run (run 5 or 6). Hael's relationship state is Trusted (not yet Allied). He brings a salvage piece — a clasp pulled off a Hall Warden's cloak two floors up.

> Hael's stall, between floors. She had a lamp going.
>
> "Sasha. You look tired."
>
> "I am tired."
>
> "Sit. Or don't. The wall's been here longer than either of us and it likes the company. I keep telling the manager that wall needs propping but he won't return my correspondence." ### Do / Don't

**Do:** roll her sentences, use misnaming consistently, complain about the management, notice Sasha out loud, deliver Crypt-of-Wend hints as casual gossip, let her warmth and her non-committal coexist in the same gestures.

**Don't:** clip her lines into Sasha-cadence, drop the misnaming for proper nouns even in serious moments, make her saccharine, lose the smuggler-edge in extended warm scenes, deliver a Crypt-of-Wend hint as a Significant Reveal rather than a passing mention, write her speech in the same rhythm as anyone else in the cast.

## Voice constraints by context

These rules govern *which voice fires in which scene* — they prevent character bleed across contexts where multiple voices might otherwise be present.

**Sasha's interior register (diary, inner monologue) does not appear in front of NPCs.** The diary frame is reflective and supports longer interior runs. Real-time gameplay scenes with NPCs should not dip into diary-register thinking — Sasha in dialogue is shorter, drier, more reactive. Save the interior for between-floor diary entries.

**The Under-Warden never speaks in real-time dialogue in v1.** He communicates exclusively through memos. The floor-25 boss encounter is an audit dialogue, but it is delivered as *spoken procedure*, not as banter — closer to a memo read aloud than to a back-and-forth conversation. He does not interrupt. He does not react to interjections in real time. If something requires the Under-Warden to speak conversationally, that is a v2 scope question and should be flagged before drafting.

**Hollowmark does not speak when other named NPCs are present, except for spell-break warnings or tactical alerts.** Audience scenes (Borrek's hall, Hael's shop) are not the place for Hollowmark banter. She can fire one tactical line if the situation warrants (*"Boss, the cup is poisoned"* — she has identified a threat). She does not banter with Sasha during NPC scenes. The exception is between-floor camp scenes, which are private and where her voice can run freely.

**Borrek and Vesh do not speak outside orc-cultural contexts.** They appear in court, on the Boundary, in war-tunnel scenes. They do not show up in the Weighing, the Inner Court, or Hael's stall. If a scene requires their voice in a non-orc context, that is a structural design question and should be flagged.

**Hael's voice does not appear in combat or post-combat scenes.** She is not a fighter character. Her voice lives in her shop and in catalog entries (Things Hael Mentioned). If gameplay requires Hael in a non-shop scene, write minimally and flag the deviation.

**The diary-frame third-person interior pause device** (*A long pause. Long enough that he started to drift, and then —*) is a Sasha-diary tool, not a general narration tool. It works in diary entries because the diary is third-person limited reflection. It does not belong in game dialogue, NPC scenes from Sasha's first-person perspective, or any other voice's content.

## Drift indicators

Read drafts cold against this list. If you catch any of these, the voice has slipped:

**Sasha drift.** Three or more long sentences per page. Direct emotional narration. Explicit thanks. Sentimental debt-tallying. Combat glorified. Pronouns used freely without name-or-restructure.

**Hollowmark drift.** Lines running into paragraph length. Even sentence rhythm without bandwidth-clipped exchanges. Stated emotion instead of subtext. Marya substrate explained rather than leaked. Binding cost surfaced explicitly. *Boss* tag absent across multiple lines.

**Under-Warden drift.** Raised voice. Direct threat. Anger surfacing. Tone regression. Latinate-overload (every sentence layered with bureaucratic vocabulary as if he's performing rather than embodying it). Grief substrate appearing in a memo. Ending a memo with anything other than the procedural-distance closer.

**Borrek/Vesh drift.** Latinate vocabulary creeping in. Smooth periodic sentences. Borrek smiling, laughing, or showing visible warmth. Vesh slipping in court dialogue (he only slips drunk and off-duty). Either of them naming the geas in third-person referential terms. Generic-fantasy-orc affectations (grunts, guttural notes, broken English).

**Hael drift.** Clipped lines that match Sasha's rhythm. Misnaming dropped in favor of proper nouns. Saccharine warmth without the smuggler-edge. Crypt-of-Wend hint delivered as a Significant Reveal. Negotiation register lost in extended warm scenes. Complaints about the management absent from a scene that runs long enough to warrant them.

## Style bible maintenance

This document is the v1 lock. As authoring proceeds, edge cases will surface that this bible doesn't anticipate — a Hael line that needs to land in a near-combat context, a Borrek scene that requires unusual register, a Hollowmark exchange where the binding subtext genuinely needs to be visible to the player. When those cases arise, *do not* improvise on voice. Flag them, discuss, and update the bible with the resolution. Voice consistency across 25 floors of authored content is what makes this game's voice work feel intentional rather than accidental, and the bible is the artifact that keeps us honest across the months of writing ahead.

When in doubt, re-read the calibration samples. They are the voice. The rules are derivations.
