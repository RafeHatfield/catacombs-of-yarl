# The Under-Warden

*A story background for an in-development mobile roguelike.*

---

## You owe a soul, and the only way to pay it is down.

You are Sasha of Reven: forty-two, small, dark-haired, with a knee that never quite forgave a job six years ago. You used to be what the trade politely called a *specialist*. You killed people for money. You stopped, not out of conscience but because the work stopped feeling like work and started feeling like the only thing you knew how to do, and that frightened you more than anyone you'd ever met across a contract.

Now you live above a vineyard you can't keep up with, you owe back-rent to a landlord who used to be a Watch sergeant and is patient but not infinitely, and you have a brass wand at your hip that knew what you were going to do before you did.

Then there's the soul. Not a figurative one. A woman named Anik died because you took a contract you should have refused, and the goddess who keeps your ledger has offered a trade: go down into the Paths of the Dead, find Anik before the bottom of the world finishes processing her, and bring her back. Do that, and your account closes.

You know the goddess isn't telling you everything. You go anyway. That's the whole game: a professional walking into a bad situation with his eyes open, noting the details, not lying to himself about what's happening. **The Under-Warden** is a 25-floor descent into a bureaucratic underworld where every fight is a problem to be solved, every NPC's exhaustion took centuries to earn, and the wand on your hip has been paying for your second chances out of her own dwindling life. She can't tell you. She isn't allowed.

You will die down there. You'll come back. Why you come back is the saddest thing in the game, and you won't understand it until you've played long enough to earn it.

---

## The world below the world

The Lady of the Long Hour is a southern Revenese goddess, smaller than the highland pantheon, more practical: the kind of god who keeps a ledger rather than a throne. She accepts contracts. She closes them. She does not love her servants; she is *fair* to them, which down here is rarer and worth more. Sasha has known her for twenty years. This is the first time she's ever offered him a way out.

Reven is south-coast country: dark-skinned, sun-cured, with its own gods, its own death-rites, and an old collective opinion that the highland afterlife doesn't technically apply to them. That opinion matters the moment Sasha steps into the Paths, which were not built for him. He's a living man with the wrong gods walking through someone else's filing system, and everyone he meets knows it.

The Paths of the Dead run twenty-five floors deep, in five regions: the **Boundary** (1-5), where the Unshriven orcs hold a four-hundred-year line; the **Dimhalls** (6-10); the **Crossing** (11-15); the **Inner Court** (16-20); and the **Weighing** (21-25), where dead souls are audited before they move on. The Paths are not empty. They are *administered*. A bureaucrat in charge. Soldiers so old they've built a culture in the dark. A smuggler running a stall between floors who has filed correspondence about a structurally unsound wall. The underworld has middle management, and middle management has opinions about you.

Sasha needs to reach the Weighing, find Anik, and get her out before the Under-Warden's machinery decides her case.

---

## Sasha, and the wand that calls him Boss

Sasha lives in texture. He notices the wet boot before he notices the danger. He calls a fight *the work*. He tracks favors the way other people track debts, which is to say constantly and without sentiment. In public he calls the wand *the wand*. In private he calls her *Hollowmark*. Only when he's frightened does he call her *Holl*, and he hates that he does.

Here he is, floor five, mid-run, writing in the diary he keeps because writing things down is how he keeps from feeling them:

> Took the long way around the cliff hall because Holl said the shaman down there was being suspicious.
>
> *He was being quiet.*
>
> Same thing.
>
> *No.*
>
> Fine. He was being quiet, which is suspicious because orcs don't shut up while they work, they sing, badly, and the shaman wasn't singing, badly or otherwise, so we took the long way and added six turns to the trip and now my left boot is wet because the long way involves a creek that nobody wrote down on any map I've seen.
>
> *Boss.*
>
> Yes.
>
> *You knew about the creek.*
>
> I knew about the creek.
>
> *You went anyway.*
>
> I went anyway. The boot dries. The shaman, presumably, was working on something that would have made it the shaman's day to ruin mine, and I would prefer the shaman not have a good day at my expense. The boot is the cheaper price. Don't make me explain economics to a wand.
>
> *I taught you economics.*
>
> Marya taught me economics. You inherited it.

That exchange is the two of them in miniature: the dry argument that's really affection, the correction (*Marya taught me*) that lands harder than it should, because Marya is who the wand used to be. The entry ends with a favor, a flask of the good stuff owed to an orc king, paid back as a piece of theater both of them will pretend is something else. "This is what passes for diplomacy down here," Sasha writes. "Two people agreeing to lie, then doing the thing."

## Hollowmark

The wand is a conscious thing of brass, and she was a person first. Marya Hollowmark was an artificer in the town of Wend: a maker of wards and portals, a fixer of broken enchantment, one of the few people anywhere who could do the truly delicate work. Seventy years ago she died, and the terms of her own craft pulled her into the wand she'd been making. The binding she'd agreed to was a *further span of service*: she would serve the next wielder until the work was done.

She is not performing Marya. She *is* Marya, living in a different body with a different job, and the seams show in small ways: present-tense ownership of places that no longer exist, people long dead, work that was hers. Here she is identifying a ring overnight, somewhere between floors eleven and twelve:

> *Carved inside the band. Worn almost smooth, but the cuts are deep enough. A name and a date.*
>
> Whose.
>
> *Not yours.*
>
> Holl.
>
> *Sorry. Habit. The name is Eshe of the Pale. The date is sixty-one years ago. The ring is a small protection ward, the household kind, the kind you give to a daughter when she leaves the house. It's still active. It shouldn't be.*
>
> Why shouldn't it be.
>
> *Because Eshe of the Pale died sixty years ago. Wards die when the warded does. This one didn't. Someone re-anchored it. Recently.*
>
> What kind of recently.
>
> *Within the year. The anchor work is — clean. Whoever did it was good. Patient.*
>
> She paused, a different kind of pause than the first one.
>
> *I don't recognize the hand. I should. I knew everyone in Wend who could do this kind of work. There weren't many of us.*

She does identification, portals, and spell-break: useful, mechanical things. The way she does them keeps catching on her own past. After the ring, a different kind of pause, then: "Wear it on your off hand. The ward will hold. If we meet who re-anchored it, I'd like to be the one to ask them how."

She is quieter than usual at the start of each new run. The player won't know why for a long time. The binding won't let her say.

## The Under-Warden

He is the most dangerous thing in the Paths, and he never raises his voice.

The Under-Warden is the bureaucrat of the dead. He runs the filing machinery that processes every soul that descends. He is not cruel, not vengeful, not hungry for anything. He operates exactly within his charter, which makes him impossible to reason with and impossible to hate cleanly. He communicates by memo, and the closer he gets to filing on you, the more polite he becomes.

Here is an early one, after Sasha's been spotted doing something irregular on floor thirteen:

> **To:** Sasha of Reven, visiting
> **Re:** Floor 13, recent visit
>
> Mr. of Reven —
>
> I trust this finds you well. [...] A small matter from your visit. The incident log notes a Hall Warden, Number 04-Kessen, who appears to have been temporarily unavailable to his post during the third bell. Number 04-Kessen reports no memory of the interval, which is — as you may appreciate — irregular for a member of his order, who are constitutionally attentive. Kessen took the omission personally.
>
> We are not, at this time, drawing conclusions. [...] No response is required at this time. Please continue to enjoy your descent.
>
> Cordially,
> The Under-Warden

And here he is later, after the pattern can no longer be ignored:

> **To:** Sasha of Reven, Specialist (ret.), of the Revenese practice, currently in unauthorized descent
> **Re:** Continued irregularities [...] with specific reference to repeated possessions of Hall Wardens
>
> The Hall Wardens of this institution are not gloves. They are not coats one removes at the door of a building one was not invited to enter. They are not vehicles. They have names, and posts, and a duty agreed to under proper rite, and they do not — and this is the point — they do not consent to intervals.
>
> I would draw your attention to the fact that this office has, on each previous occasion, declined to escalate. That was a courtesy. Courtesies are not entitlements.
>
> A formal complaint is now on file. [...] It is filed.
>
> Regards,
> The Under-Warden

He carries one grief, and he will never tell you about it. A woman passed through the Paths eighty years ago and he never asked after her. The grief surfaces a single time, in a mural on floor nineteen: Hollowmark reads it aloud, Sasha says nothing, and the player understands. The game never confirms it. By the time you reach his office, you'll have an opinion about him, and it won't be the simple one.

## King Borrek and Captain Vesh — the Unshriven

Four hundred years ago, a company of orc-soldiers died without finishing their duty. The first Warden reincarnated them into orc-bodies as a judgment dressed up as a job: hold the Boundary against the deeper undead. They have held for four centuries. What the binding quietly also did was forbid them from *advancing* the line. They can defend. They cannot push. Four hundred years of holding without gaining a single pace is a specific and terrible kind of tired, and they know it.

The geas constrains even what they're permitted to *ask aloud*, which is why Sasha, Revenese and bound by none of their rites, interests them so much. He can do things they can't ask for.

One run, he moves a boundary marker four paces deeper. Nobody asked. King Borrek, on a throne of bones older than any orc in the room, summons him:

> "On the floor below this one," Borrek said, "there is a marker. A standing stone. [...] You noticed it, and then on your return upward, the marker was four paces further down the corridor than it had been."
>
> "Four paces, yes."
>
> "Four paces is not a great distance. Stones do not move on their own. I am told this by people who would know."
>
> I waited. [...] "I will not ask how the stone came to stand where it stands. I will say only this: it stands well there. Better than where it stood. We do not, ourselves, agree to anything in particular about the matter."
>
> Vesh shifted then. One short word, low: "It's a good place for it."
>
> Borrek did not look at him. He did not need to. "Captain Vesh is correct. It is a good place for it."

Vesh almost slips; Borrek covers without a glance. The whole conversation is a wall of things they're not allowed to say, and underneath it, men who would give anything for the line to move and cannot ask anyone to move it. The scene ends with borr-lath set aside at the door, "a courtesy of the hall." That's how gratitude works when you're forbidden from feeling it out loud.

After enough favors across enough runs, Borrek looks at Sasha differently. Not friendlier; he was always correct. More honest. Then the flask, then news of his daughter's bloodline, and Sasha finally sees what four hundred years of holding a line looks like on a man who can never advance it.

## Hael, the smuggler — the one who notices you

Everyone in the Paths is tired, procedural, or buried under a geas. Hael is none of these. She runs a stall between floors, warmer and longer-breathed than anything else down here, and the warmth is *chosen*, which means there's something underneath it; nobody operates a black market in the underworld by being soft. She calls the Under-Warden *the manager*, the Hall Wardens *the boys upstairs*, the orcs *the boys downstairs*.

> "Sasha. You look tired."
>
> "I am tired."
>
> "Sit. Or don't. The wall's been here longer than either of us and it likes the company. I keep telling the manager that wall needs propping but he won't return my correspondence." [...] "Off one of the boys upstairs. Fourteen, north wing — they're the only ones still issuing the old style this cycle. I'm right, aren't I."
>
> "You're right."
>
> "Of course I am."

Hael *notices Sasha*. Out loud, by name. Nobody else in the Paths does: to the Under-Warden he's a case number, to the orcs a useful anomaly, to the goddess a ledger entry. She watches his eyes when she mentions the Weighing and tells him so. "You came down faster today. Something pushing you." She is the closest the game comes to offering the player a friend, and she's also the one quietly seeding the route to the strangest ending in the game: a Wend-place down past the audit floor that the boys downstairs won't go near, that she's stopped asking about because the asking offends them in ways she can't predict. "I priced it fair," she says of a salvaged clasp. "I always price things fair the third time." Even her kindness keeps a ledger. That's why you trust it.

## What you'll remember

Other roguelikes give you a build. This one gives you *stories you tell afterward*: moments the systems made possible but never announced.

**The time I beat the troll with an acid trap.** Around floor seven. The wand doesn't lecture; she *mentions* things. A hazard here, a property there. Then you're standing in a room with a troll and an acid pool, you remember what she mentioned, and the troll stops being a wall and becomes a lever. The game never told you that interaction existed. It trusted you to find it.

**The time I wore a Hall Warden to the altar.** Around floor thirteen. You can leave your own body bleeding on the floor and step into an enemy's, and a Warden's body *knows the Paths* in ways your living one never will. Sasha does not love this; it's the kind of thing the old work made too easy. He uses it anyway, and the Under-Warden notices, and files.

**The time Hollowmark told me the orc on the other side of the portal had a cursed axe.** She can't see through walls, but she can *feel* curses. She told him before he opened the portal. He didn't listen. The axe was, indeed, cursed. The wand is right more often than Sasha admits, and the game lets you ignore her and pay for it.

**The time I drank borr-lath with King Borrek and he gave me his daughter's bloodline news.** Not a quest reward: a relationship reaching, after runs of small unasked favors, the only intimacy four centuries of geas will permit. An old king being honest with you for one flask's length, because you did the thing he couldn't ask for.

**The time I broke the Under-Warden's grip on my own corpse.** You die. You come back. The version of you that died is still down there, and the Under-Warden *filed it*. Your old body now walks Warden-patrol, conscripted. The wand can break possession. She breaks it. The body drops. She says: *"That wasn't you anymore, Boss. You can have the gear back now."* The philosophical premise of the ending, one continued existence traded for another, is in your hands as a single mechanical interaction long before anyone says the word out loud.

## What's on the line

Three ways to win, none of them clean.

**The Clean Audit.** You carry Stones of Intuition across twenty-five floors, each one a record of a moment you did the harder right thing, and present them as evidence of good conduct until the Under-Warden closes the file. Anik is released by procedure. He notes the irregularity of a living visitor conducting his own audit and flags it for future review. No one hugs. The ledger closes. The lawful ending, and it costs you every difficult choice it took to fill that bag of stones.

**The Theft.** You find Anik before the Weighing and pull her out through the Crypt of Wend, a hidden floor reachable only by the smuggler's route Hael has been hinting at, run after run. You just walk out. The Under-Warden's final memo isn't even addressed to you. It's addressed to the file: *"The visitor has departed. The matter remains open. The matter will remain open."* You didn't beat him. You left while he was still filing. He will be filing forever.

**The Swap.** Hael knows about this one and will never say it directly, because to choose it you have to understand what Hollowmark *is* and what she's been doing. At the Weighing, standing in front of Anik, you can trade places: your soul for hers. Not death, but whatever the Paths do with a willing exchange. Hollowmark says: *"I always knew, Boss. I never minded."*

Here's what makes that line detonate. Sasha keeps coming back from death not by grace or luck but by the binding. *A further span of service* means the wielder gets pulled back to the work when the work isn't finished, and every time Hollowmark hauls Sasha out of the Paths and back to his bed above the vineyard, she spends some of her own remaining span to do it. Run after run, for however many it took. She can't tell him; the binding forbids it. Marya knew what she was agreeing to seventy years ago. She agreed anyway.

The player who's pieced this together (the present-tense slips, the re-anchored ward she half-recognized, the quiet at the start of each descent) hears *"I always knew, Boss. I never minded"* not as comfort but as confession. She has been giving up her own time to give him his, the whole way down.

You owe a soul. You go down to pay it. Somewhere in the dark, the wand at your hip has been quietly paying a debt of her own, one you never knew you'd run up, and the only way you find out is to play long enough to deserve the knowing.
