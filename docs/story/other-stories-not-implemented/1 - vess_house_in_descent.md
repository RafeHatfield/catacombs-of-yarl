# The house remembers her: a proposed story for Catacombs of Yarl

**Kill the dragon.** The current premise scores two on any reasonable "world with an idea" checklist — Zhyraxion and Aurelyn are placeholders wearing sentiment as costume jewelry, and the player never met either of them, so the Ruby Heart is an emotional IOU the story can't cash. The gameplay loop is good; the story has to earn the loop, not decorate it. What follows is the concrete, opinionated replacement — a single premise with everything downstream of it solved, built to reuse every system already in the engine. The dev said "rip it up if you have a killer idea." I think this is one.

The headline: **you are a hired specialist sent into a retired magician's grief-rotted house to retrieve what is left of his wife, who died in an accident eleven years ago and is still, in pieces, somewhere on the lower floors.** The magician makes you tea on level zero. The house tries to kill you because the house is half-her now, and she has opinions. Every time you die, the guild sends the next specialist, and the magician writes a letter of apology to your next of kin, with your unpaid wages enclosed. That is the whole pitch.

It fixes the four things the current draft cannot fix without replacement: the villain is specific and present-tense (you meet him in the first room of every run), the MacGuffin is emotionally concrete (a letter, not a gem), the death-and-reset is a contract shift rather than a cosmological conceit, and the descent is into **someone's actual life** instead of a generic 25-story dungeon. It keeps everything the dev has built — signposts, murals, vaults, assassins, portals, heirlooms, the Guide, the wet-shoes joke — and only changes what they *mean*.

## A) The pitch in one breath

*Orren Vess, a retired and catastrophic magician, hires professionals to retrieve his dead wife from the lower floors of his own house, which has been rearranging itself to keep her from him for eleven years. You are the latest hire. She is not the MacGuffin. She is the villain.*

That last sentence is the twist the premise is built around, and it's the move that resolves the Gruber-vs-Snape problem cleanly. The **client is not the villain**. Orren is warm, polite, devastated, and 100% the reason this is happening — but he is not the voice you are fighting. He is your employer, your correspondent, and eventually your moral problem. The **villain** is Marsya, or what remains of her, speaking through the house she has become: bored, theatrical, specifically funny, cosmically wounded, territorially OP because **she is the geography**. She cannot leave the citadel because she **is** the citadel. The dev's hardest staging problem — a villain bound to a place — becomes the premise's strongest idea instead of its weakest excuse.

## B) The PC: a specialist under contract, named Kel

The Vlad Taltos voice only works if the PC is a **working professional with a specific small-scale reason to be down there**, not a hero. Make the PC a guild-contracted problem-solver — the Ingersoll Guild hires them out to magicians, bureaucrats, and widowers who have household situations they can't personally resolve. Down is a job. Up is a paycheck. The wry register is free: **every reluctant-hero story is structurally a mismatch between the protagonist's stated small goal and the plot's insisted-on large one**, and a specialist-for-hire already comes with a small goal (finish the contract, collect the fee, don't die on Tuesday).

Name them **Kel**. One syllable, low-status, no surname given. A fixed name, not player-entered — player-entered names break a distinctive diary voice, and nobody wants to read Brust in the voice of xxDragonSlayer420xx. Kel is a person. The grandfather-figure NPC at the hub calls them Kel; the villain eventually calls them Kel; enemies don't. The name is earned in use.

Why they descend: **they inherited the contract from their mentor**, who took the Vess job eight years ago and didn't come back. Kel owes the Guild for their training, the mentor left them a small estate of debts, and the Vess contract is the one with hazard pay that actually clears the ledger. They took the job because it paid. They are staying because, increasingly, they're curious about what happened to their mentor. They are not here to save anyone. They will, probably, save someone. They will complain about it.

Agency under the swept-along constraint: resolve this the Disco Elysium way. Kel is pulled by the contract (unnegotiable, the plot's demand) and the player shapes **how they react** to being pulled — which optional rooms they enter, which side-quests they grudgingly take (\"fine, I'll look for your stupid ring, but this is costing you\"), which assassins they spare, whether they read Marsya's letters before handing them over. The stated preference stays consistent (finish the job, leave); the accumulation of small choices is the characterization.

**What a Kel diary entry sounds like** (target register — short, Chandler-meets-Pratchett, one practical detail per beat):

> *Day six. The stairs down from the drawing room go three floors farther than the drawing room has any business permitting. Grandfather always said the first rule of a contract was to finish it and the second was to get paid; I'm currently one for two. Mord sent down fresh chalk, a pot of tea, and a note that under Clause 14b hazard pay now retroactively applies to \"household residents currently or formerly spouse-adjacent to the client.\" I don't know what that means. Mord doesn't either. The tea is still warm. I'm choosing to find this companionable.*

> *Found the parlor today. Willow growing through the carpet. Portrait had turned its face to the wall, which was polite of it. The previous specialist was in the hall cupboard. I took her boots. They fit. I'm not going to think about that.*

**Item flavor, same voice:**

> *Rusted shortsword. Previous owner discouraged. Will probably work one more time before it files a grievance.*

> *Potion, yellow. Tastes like bad wine and worse apologies. Closes holes. Does not ask where the holes came from, which is the mark of a professional.*

> *Marsya's reading spectacles. One lens cracked in a specific pattern. I can see through it perfectly well, which is the problem.*

The rule across every fragment: **one joke per beat, never in the same beat as real damage**. When Kel takes a serious hit or sees something genuinely wrong, the voice goes flat and descriptive; the aside comes later, once they're moving again. That's the Arthur Morgan journal discipline and it's what separates this voice from Marvel quip-creep.

## C) The villain: Marsya, speaking through the house

Here is the whole move, plainly. Orren thinks he is trying to rescue his wife. The lower floors of the house disagree with his framing. **What grew out of the accident isn't a ghost of Marsya and isn't not-her either — it is the part of Marsya that has been eleven years alone with what he did, and it has opinions, and it has been redecorating.** She calls herself the Remainder in chalk. She speaks with the theatrical, amused, proprietary register of someone who has been the sovereign of a specific twenty-five-floor kingdom for over a decade and finds her husband's hireable specialists charming, briefly, the way a cat finds a bird charming.

This solves everything the dev was worried about. The villain is OP because **the dungeon is her body**. She is bored because her husband keeps sending people and they keep being insufficient. She is kinetic without leaving because her reach is architectural — she rearranges floors, seals doors, writes on walls, commands the previous specialists who did not come home, and dispatches named assassins from her household staff. The grief is real and hidden and landable because it is the grief of **a specific woman about a specific night**, and the player assembles it from fragments of her life before the accident, not from her monologues about it.

She is not Snape. She does not mope. Her voice is **Gruber's specificity, Handsome Jack's enthusiastic charm, GLaDOS's escalating-politeness, and Alan Rickman's Sheriff-of-Nottingham register-shift** — bureaucratic verbs applied to appalling things. She is at her most entertaining when her plans are failing. Five sample voice lines, written for tap-to-dismiss on mobile, each under twenty words where possible, each using concrete nouns:

> *First address, floor 1 mural, after Kel's first kill:*
> **\"Oh. You made it past the parlor. Orren's been bragging about you. He bragged about the last one too. I kept her coat.\"**

> *Mid-dungeon intrusion, full-screen, after Kel breaks something she cares about:*
> **\"That mirror was a wedding gift. I hated it for eleven years. I was going to break it myself next Tuesday. You've spoiled a small pleasure. I'll have to find another.\"**

> *Mocking the MacGuffin quest, signpost deeper down:*
> **\"You're looking for my letter. It's adorable. I wrote it. I know where it is. I'll give it to him myself, personally, the moment he comes down and asks. Tell him that. Tell him exactly that.\"**

> *Complimenting Kel's violence, after a named assassin falls:*
> **\"Vey is dead. You used a chair. I would applaud but my hands are the hallway. Consider this the applause.\"**

> *Grief leaking for one sentence, overheard fragment the player finds in an assassin's correspondence — never in dialogue aimed at Kel:*
> **\"Tell Torren his letters are adequate. No — tell him they're good. He is the only one who writes me anymore. Anyway. Kill her on fourteen.\"**

Notice what these do. They assume intimacy. They name specific objects (coat, mirror, Tuesday, chair, hallway). They register-shift from bureaucratic to domestic to cruel and back. They flirt with the fourth wall — she knows Kel keeps coming back — without ever saying the word \"roguelike.\" And none of them whine. The **hurt is always a pressure leak, never an announcement**. The rule, borrowed from Handsome Jack's Angel arc and Hannibal's Mischa silence: *the villain's wound is everywhere in the level architecture and nowhere in her mouth*.

**What she does kinetically across the descent:**

*The Throne Voice* fires rarely — three or four full-screen intrusions across a run, at floor transitions. *The Intercom* fires short: one-line ambient blurts when Kel clears a room, finds a specific item, or returns to a floor after dying there. *The Record* is her correspondence — assassin contracts, notes to Orren she never sent, marginalia in books, the chalk she writes over his chalk, the changes she's made to the maps he drew. *The Sign* is the physical signature — one specific flower (let's call it *nightlace*) that grows where she's paid attention, a recurring emblem she has scratched into doorframes, a smell Kel notes in inscription text. All four scale cheaply because they're text in a specific typographic voice over the dev's existing systems.

The **assassins are her household**. Not hired guns from outside — the servants, guests, and specialists who were in the house when the accident happened and never left, plus the professionals Orren has sent down over eleven years who she has turned one by one. The dev builds **five named antagonists** whose dialogue extends the villain's presence through the dungeon without Marsya ever appearing in person until the finale:

- **Countess Vey** (early, floors 3–5): a bored aristocrat who was visiting the night it happened, now running the upper-floor chokepoints because it amuses her. Gruber-parallel. Leaves calling cards.
- **Brask the Inch** (mid, floors 7–10): the brutal one, formerly a household guard. Short lines, physical register. Says \"I was getting bored\" when Kel arrives.
- **Lyra the Cartographer** (mid-deep, floors 12–15): never fights Kel directly — seals doors, moves corridors, leaves traps. Her correspondence with Marsya is flirtatious and argumentative; she is the character who most reveals Marsya is *teasable, peer-able, a person*. When Kel finally meets her on 15, she's packed to leave. She's the one who tells Kel, flatly, that Marsya changed.
- **Torren the Fourth Son** (deep, 18–20): a true believer specialist who thinks Marsya is holy. His journals are devoted. Marsya's notes about him, found later, are cruel, which is the game's hardest moment — the villain's contempt for the one person who loves her.
- **The First Specialist** (endgame, 22–24): Kel's mentor. Still here. Still moving. The game's earned pathos-bridge. The mentor is not evil; they are tired. They do not monologue. They fight reluctantly and they say, before falling, one sentence about how they should have turned back on floor four.

Each assassin has an intro bark, three combat taunts keyed to Kel's health, a defeat line, a post-defeat fragment found on re-examination, and one late-game reference from Marsya herself (\"*you killed Vey — I owed her nothing but I miss her taste*\"). That's about fifty lines total across the five. One good writing week.

**When and how grief lands.** Tiered across the 25 floors. Floors 1–5 establish Marsya as bored and sovereign, the winning side. Floors 6–10 start introducing non-Marsya voices — servants, guests, the younger Orren — and one mural shows a feast with a woman's face unclear. Floors 11–15 deliver contradictions: servants' diaries describing \"the accident,\" \"the betrayal,\" \"the loss,\" three different events, none fully resolved. Floors 16–20 deliver the shape: Marsya was a working witch, Orren was her senior and her husband, she was the better magician, the accident was not an accident in the way Orren's chalk says it was. **The player never learns what happened from Marsya.** They learn it from servant diaries, from Lyra's correspondence, from a torn page Orren keeps redrafting, from the architecture of the nursery that was built for a child that never came. And crucially, **Marsya's own voice in this range does not acknowledge the pattern the fragments are forming** — she keeps joking, keeps mocking, keeps being specific about wine and chairs — which is what makes her voice tragic instead of pitiable. She is the woman who, even dying, will not dignify the unspeakable room by naming it.

## D) The world: the Vess House and the Ingersoll Guild

The setting is **one house**, not a dungeon. It is called the Vess House. It is on a hill outside a nondescript small city (name it something boring — **Halvern**, **Colfet**, **Dreywich**, pick one and don't spend budget on it). It has a front door, a parlor, a kitchen, a drawing room, a library, a conservatory — and then, past a specific staircase that only admits specialists, it has twenty-five more floors, which are getting worse.

The **one weird idea** that carries the whole setting: *the lower floors of this house are a woman's grief made out of household objects, and each floor is further into her life with Orren and further back in their marriage*. The deeper Kel goes, the **younger** the wallpaper is. Floor 2 is the house as it was when Orren retired. Floor 10 is the house the year they bought it. Floor 20 is the house of her childhood, reconstructed from memory. Floor 25 is the one room she will not let anyone see. This gives the dev twenty-five floors of **biographical descent** using only Oryx's domestic tileset — parlors, studies, nurseries, bedrooms, conservatories, wine cellars, libraries, flooded halls.

The **one specific material** the world runs on is **household magic** — Marsya was a specialist in domestic sorcery, the magic of *things in use*, which is why the house can weaponize a dresser and why every item in the game has a pedigree. Potions are her recipes. Wands are her husband's trade tools. Scrolls are letters. This is the Brust move — **magic as a trade, not a mystery** — and it grounds every consumable. You are not carrying the Aegis of Eternal Vigil; you are carrying **Mord's Spare Lantern** and **Letter 7 of 12** and **A Bootprint in Wax**.

The **one specific economy** is the Ingersoll Guild's contract system. Specialists are indentured under standard terms. Deaths incur recovery fees. Wages are docked for gear lost. Hazard pay is negotiated per clause. The clerk, **Parrin Mord**, sends you memos. This is the cheapest way to make the world feel *lived-in bureaucratically* — every piece of paperwork is worldbuilding, and it generates Pratchett humor for free.

**Names, stripped of fantasy placeholder:**

- *People:* Orren Vess, Marsya Vess-Halder, Parrin Mord, Kel, Countess Vey, Brask the Inch, Lyra the Cartographer, Torren the Fourth Son
- *Places:* the Vess House, the Long Parlor, the Green Staircase, the Garden Under, the Flooded Library, the Bone Aviary, the Nursery That Was Not Used
- *Factions:* the Ingersoll Guild, the Household (Marsya's staff, living and otherwise)
- *Items:* Mord's Spare Lantern, the Contract (Signed), Letter 7 of 12, the Remainder's Hairpin, Marsya's Reading Spectacles
- *The game's title:* **The Remainder** — or, if the dev wants something more verbose and Piranesi-adjacent, **The Vess House, In Descent**. \"Catacombs of Yarl\" goes in the bin. \"Zhyraxion,\" \"Aurelyn,\" \"Crimson Order,\" and \"Amulet of Yendor\" all go with it.

**What players will screenshot** (section K has the full list, but the world-establishing one is this): **the tea scene**. Level zero, a wooden table, two teacups. One is for Kel. The other is for the specialist before Kel, whose body is not yet home. The second cup will stay steaming, untouched, when Kel walks to the stairs. That is the first frame of the game. That is its cover art.

## E) The MacGuffin: Marsya's letter, which she wrote on the night it happened

Not a heart. Not a gem. **A letter she wrote to Orren on the night of the accident, which she never sent, and which has been moving around the lower floors of the house for eleven years, because she can't decide whether she wants him to read it.** The player assembles it page by page — Letter 1 of 12, Letter 2, and so on — across runs. The final page is on floor 25. You cannot get all twelve pages in one run. That is the entire replay engine.

Why Orren can't just go get it himself: because **the house is her and she will not let him in**. He can enter floors 0–1 with difficulty and floor 2 only briefly. Beyond that, the house recognizes him and refuses. This is why he hires specialists — they are strangers to the house and the house has to *learn* them before it can reject them, which takes on average twenty-to-forty minutes of real-world gameplay per specialist. It is a brutally functional diegetic reason that is also the game's core loop: **the house is getting better at recognizing you**.

Why it matters emotionally: Kel reads the letter. Once, in full, on the final floor, before delivering it. The player chooses whether they read it before Orren does, and whether they deliver it at all. This is the ending-differentiator, not three separate boss fights. See section I.

## F) Death-and-reset: the Contract

**Kill the soul-rotation conceit.** \"Entity collects souls\" is Hades with the serial numbers filed off, and it makes the PC disposable in a way that kneecaps player attachment. Replace it with the **Ingersoll Guild's standard specialist contract**, which stipulates that on specialist death the Guild recovers remains and gear, forwards wages (minus recovery fee) to next of kin, and dispatches the next specialist on the rotation. You are not the same soul. **You are the next hire.** Your diary from the previous run is in the footlocker at the front door; you can read it. Your mentor's diary is there too, from eight years ago.

This is the best possible replacement for four reasons. (1) It is **Brust-compatible** — Vlad Taltos would literally take this contract. (2) It is **session-agnostic** — a run is a shift, and shifts end and begin without ceremony, which is the mobile constraint. (3) It justifies gear inheritance diegetically — the Guild recovers and reissues. (4) It transforms the Guide from Mysterious Ghost into **Parrin Mord, a nervous clerk with a clipboard**, which is an enormous tonal upgrade and generates comedy on every contact.

What you lose: the \"you are secretly the villain\" twist. What you gain: twenty small twists (your predecessor left a warning, the villain writes you a note, the clerk is adjusting clauses behind the Guild's back, your mentor's contract is still active and you find their body still working) — which is the currency roguelike fragment storytelling actually trades in.

## G) Story delivery architecture: six layers over existing systems

The dev has already built the pipes. This is what runs through them.

**Layer 1, ambient (every tap): signposts and murals as canonical voices.** The 90+ existing signposts get rewritten as **in-world artifacts with identified authors**: most are Orren's chalk (helpful on early floors, cryptic on mid, pleading on deep, and on floor 25 written in Marsya's hand). A few are servant graffiti, specialist graffiti (Kel's predecessors), or Marsya's own chalk written *over* Orren's. Murals are the Household's visual record of the Vess marriage — forty hand-written at ~40 words each, one every four or five floors, including four passes at the Mural of the Accident that resolves more clearly with each viewing.

**Layer 2, pickup (optional): every consumable gets one sentence of pedigree.** Scrolls become letters with titles (\"Scroll of the Fifth Watch\"). Potions name their vintner. Weapons name their previous owner. About sixty items × two sentences = three days of concentrated writing. This is the single highest-ROI writing task in the project.

**Layer 3, spaces (rooms): vaults as named places.** The vault system's ten identities, tuned to this premise: the Scholar's Library (Orren's study at different ages), the Warden's Quarters (a specialist's foxhole), the Alchemist's Workshop (Marsya's conservatory), the Ossuary, the Pauper's Hoard, the Cartographer's Nook (Lyra's domain), the Wet Shrine (home of the wet-shoes portal joke, now a fountain Marsya uses to send Orren messages), the Tax Collector's Strongroom (the Guild's field office, locked, with back-wages in it), the Assassin's Safehouse (where a specialist died), and the Heir's Chamber (the Nursery That Was Not Used — the room you don't expect and never recover from). Each vault is a name + identity + loot family + one signature inscription + one reactive flag. Ten vaults × two hours each = one writing week.

**Layer 4, characters: NPCs, merchants, and the Guide.** Parrin Mord replaces the Guide. He appears at pre-arranged floor safe-rooms (replacing the old 5/10/15/20 cadence but with **varying appearances** rather than fixed ones — sometimes he's on 6, sometimes he skips 10 because the Guild is auditing him). He has a clipboard, a thermos, a benefits package, and thirty lines of Pratchett-clerical register. A hub merchant, a grandfather-figure at the Guild house, the specialist footlocker, three secret-room NPCs. About a hundred hub lines total.

**Layer 5, antagonists: the five named assassins** as detailed in section C, each with roughly ten lines (intro, three combat, defeat, post-mortem fragment, late-villain-reference). Fifty lines.

**Layer 6, meta: heirlooms as keepsakes, the Codex, and reactive flags.** Each persistent hub NPC gives one heirloom after rapport, mechanically a stat tweak and narratively a \"thinking of you\" signal that unlocks reactive dialogue from *other* NPCs who notice. The Codex is a scrollable journal, written in Mord's voice as the Guild's official case file on the Vess Contract, unlocked by natural play. And **reactive dialogue is driven by a single `HashSet<string>` of persistent flags and a priority-weighted line pool** — the entire Hades architecture, stripped of voice acting and branching trees, implementable in two days of engineering. Threshold-gated lines (`persistent.Count > 15` triggers Mord's rueful tier, `> 30` triggers his haunted tier) do 80% of the work at 10% of the authoring cost of unique-flag-gated lines.

Total narrative writing: about 15,000 words. A novella. Three to four months of a 6–12 month schedule, interleaved with mechanical work, not blocked.

## H) Replay deepening: what changes across runs

Concrete examples, all implementable with the flag system above.

**Letter 7 of 12 and its siblings.** The twelve pages of Marsya's letter are scattered across the 25 floors, but only three to five can be collected in any single run because of vault-spawn weighting and the fact that some pages are in rooms that only appear after specific flags trigger. You will need at least four full runs to collect all twelve. On run N, if the player has letters 1, 4, and 9, Mord's dialogue references exactly that subset (\"*You've got the one where she apologizes and the one where she doesn't. I'd be cautious of the middle pages, Specialist, they get worse*\").

**Mord gets tireder.** Persistent run count thresholds shift his tone. First five runs: polite, procedural, mildly apologetic. Runs 6–15: familiar, wry, starts sharing biscuits. Runs 16+: confides. Eventually tells Kel that he has been Orren's clerk for a long time and that the Guild's contract with Mr. Vess is older than Mord's own tenure. Eventually tells Kel about the clause he has been quietly omitting.

**Orren learns Kel's name.** First encounter: \"Specialist.\" After floor 5 cleared once: \"Specialist Kel.\" After ten runs: \"Kel.\" After the player finds Orren's hidden draft-letter to the Guild asking to **cancel the contract**: Orren stops saying their name, because he knows what it means.

**The assassins remember.** If Kel evaded Vey on the previous run, Vey's intro line this run is \"*You ran. That was the correct choice last week. It is not the correct choice this week.*\" If Kel killed Brask using a specific unusual weapon, Lyra's correspondence this run references it wryly. If Kel spared Torren, Torren reappears on a later floor, escorted by household silences, having been *demoted* by Marsya.

**The house changes.** Persistent flag: `player_has_read_letter_N`. Each letter-page read permanently changes one room somewhere else in the house — a portrait's face resolves, a door appears, a locked vault unlocks. The house is **responding to what Kel knows**, because Marsya is.

**The mentor's body advances.** The First Specialist (Kel's mentor) is on floor 22 in early runs. As Kel gets closer across runs, the mentor moves *up* the house, to 20, to 18, to 15. By the time Kel is good enough to reach them, they are closer than they should be, which is Marsya letting them meet. This costs one flag and one spawn-table adjustment.

## I) Endings: one ending delivered differently, on three axes

**Build one ending, stitched from flag-gated paragraphs, not three separate boss fights.** Hades proved this works and the combinatorial cost savings are enormous. The one ending is: Kel stands in Marsya's childhood room on floor 25, with as much of the letter as they have assembled, and the Remainder speaks to them without the house's theatrics for the first time. Then Kel leaves. The epilogue text is stitched from paragraphs keyed to three axes.

- **Axis 1: did you read the letter yourself before delivering it?** If yes, Kel knows what it says and their diary's final entry acknowledges it. If no, they don't, and Orren's reaction to receiving it is the player's only window into the contents. Both are painful in different directions.
- **Axis 2: did you deliver it to Orren, keep it, or burn it?** Deliver: Orren reads, disintegrates, thanks Kel in a letter that arrives a year later via Mord. Keep: Kel goes home carrying a piece of a woman they will think about for the rest of their life. Burn: the house goes silent, Marsya free in a way that is also an ending for her, Orren never knows.
- **Axis 3: what did you do with the First Specialist?** Killed: Mord's epilogue letter mentions the mentor's pension paid out. Spared/escorted up: the mentor reappears at the Guild house, changed, and has one scene with Kel. Left behind: the mentor is still down there; you can visit in New Game Plus.

Three axes × roughly three states each = 27 possible epilogue stitches from about 20 paragraphs of authored text. That is **\"genuinely distinct endings\"** without \"same fight at three difficulties.\" The emotional core of each axis is clean: axis 1 is about whether you trespassed on someone's grief; axis 2 is about what you owe your employer versus what you owe the dead; axis 3 is about professional obligation and friendship. The dev's original six-ending list compresses into this cleanly — *keep heart + fight* becomes *keep letter + refuse delivery*; *give heart* becomes *deliver letter unread*; *destroy heart with true name* becomes *burn letter after reading*; the secret dark ending becomes *find the twelfth page, which reveals Orren wrote it in her handwriting*.

## J) Keep versus replace, specifically

**Keep:** the fragment-delivery architecture (signposts, murals, tap-examine). It's the best thing in the codebase and it is exactly the right pipe for this story. Keep the vault system and convert its contents per section G. Keep the planned assassins system and promote it per section C. Keep the portal system and the wet-shoes joke (it becomes something Mord apologizes for on company letterhead, which is funnier than it was). Keep the heirloom/persistent-NPC plan and treat heirlooms as keepsakes per Hades. Keep the Guide character **as a character** (not as a cutscene delivery mechanism) by reconceiving them as Mord. Keep the 25-floor descent structure — it maps cleanly onto biographical depth.

**Replace, with reasoning:** The dragon premise (Zhyraxion, Aurelyn) — replace, because it scores two on the world-with-an-idea checklist and the villain's grief is inaccessible to the player because the player never met either dragon. The Crimson Order — replace with the Household and specialist predecessors, because \"hooded cultists\" carries no specific wound and \"servants-turned-over-eleven-years\" does. The Ruby Heart — replace with Marsya's letter, because a letter is emotionally concrete and assemblable-across-runs. Soul rotation — replace with the Ingersoll contract, because contracts generate comedy, justify gear inheritance, and are Brust-compatible. The Guide's fixed-depth monologues — replace with Mord's irregular clerical visits plus the Guide's voice distributed across the mural and Codex systems. The Amulet of Yendor placeholder — drop it entirely; the NetHack reverence is the wrong register. \"Catacombs of Yarl\" — rename, per section D.

**Don't be mealy-mouthed about this.** The dragon premise is weak specifically because it's a placeholder stack — every noun is the first noun dark fantasy reaches for — and no amount of polish will make the player care about a dragon couple they never met. The house premise is strong specifically because the player meets Orren on every run, reads Marsya's letters across runs, and walks through the rooms of their actual marriage. The emotional contract is signed in the first five minutes and audited across forty hours.

## K) Signature images and moments

Five scenes that should live in a player's memory for years.

- **The two teacups.** Level zero. Orren sets out two cups, one for Kel, one for the specialist before Kel. The second stays steaming, untouched, as Kel walks to the stairs. This frame is the cover art.
- **The letter wall.** One floor is a corridor whose walls are papered in opened envelopes — Orren's correspondence with every specialist who came before Kel, including the draft letter to Kel's mother that Orren writes while Kel is alive. Kel can read their own obituary in draft form.
- **The Nursery That Was Not Used.** A perfectly prepared child's room, dusty but undisturbed, on a floor the house really did not want Kel on. No combat. One hairpin on the floor. Kel can pick it up. It has no stats. That is the point.
- **The Remainder glimpsed, once.** Mid-deep floor, a figure through a doorway brushing her hair. When Kel approaches, the room is empty and the hairpin is on the floor and the mirror is cracked in a specific pattern and Kel's reading spectacles (if they have them) now match the crack exactly.
- **Mord on the landing.** Somewhere around floor 15, Mord is sitting on a staircase with his clipboard and a thermos of tea he brought from home, because the Guild has decided the contract is getting complicated and he has been sent to renegotiate Clause 14b in person. He is terrified and trying very hard to be professional about it. He has biscuits. Kel can have one.

Honorable mention, which might replace the Nursery depending on how dark the dev wants to go: **the Mural of the Accident**, encountered four times at increasing resolution, until on the final viewing it is clearly Marsya and clearly also the house and clearly Orren with his hand on something he shouldn't be touching. **That's** the screenshot friends describe to friends.

## L) Scope and sequencing for a solo dev

Realistic in 6–12 months, in build order.

**Month 1–2: the spine.** Rewrite the premise in all existing content. Rename everything — Vess, Marsya, Mord, Kel, the Ingersoll Guild, the Vess House. Retarget the 90 existing signposts as Orren's chalk on shallow floors, servant graffiti on mid floors, Marsya's chalk on deep floors. Rewrite the four murals. Build the flag system (two days) and the priority-weighted line pool (two days). Write Mord's ~30 lines and the Guild hub.

**Month 3–4: the villain and her household.** Write Marsya's voice across the four channels (Throne, Intercom, Record, Sign). Build the five named assassins, each with ten lines and an AI quirk (reskin existing enemies — don't build new behaviors). Wire the assassins' memory flags. Write the Mural of the Accident's four resolution passes.

**Month 5: the vaults as places.** Convert ten vault identities per section G. Write per-vault inscriptions and loot pedigrees. This is the single cheapest and highest-impact content block.

**Month 6: the letter.** Write twelve pages of Marsya's letter. Distribute across floors with spawn weighting. Wire the reactive flags. Write the epilogue's ~20 flag-gated paragraphs.

**Month 7–9 (if available): meta and polish.** Heirlooms as keepsakes across persistent NPCs. The Codex. Replay-deepening lines for Mord, Orren, and the hub merchant. The First Specialist — Kel's mentor — as a late-game encounter with branching outcomes.

**Months 10–12: the trim.** Cut what doesn't land. Playtest for text density on phone. Tune the letter-collection pacing (target: four-to-six runs to assemble all twelve pages).

**Cut first if scope tightens:** voice acting (never was in scope), any Ambrosia-style secondary gift currency (collapse to one keepsake per NPC), branching dialogue trees (use priority-weighted pools), faction politics (there is no faction system — the Household is not a faction, it is a villain), procedural history generation (never attempt what Qud did; write three perspectives on one event and stop), Nemesis-style emergent rivals (five hand-written named assassins is the entire budget).

**Minimum viable story if the dev has to ship fast:** Orren in the opening room, Mord as clerk-guide, three named assassins instead of five, six letter pages instead of twelve, the vault-as-place system at seven identities, one ending stitched on one axis (the letter: deliver or burn). That ships a game whose story is already better than 90% of roguelikes, in under six months.

## Conclusion

The dragon premise is a placeholder that has been keeping the seat warm for the actual story, which is about a man who killed his wife by accident eleven years ago and has been hiring strangers to retrieve what's left of her because the house she has become will not let him in. That story is specific, Pratchett-funny, Brust-voiced, Hades-structured, and fits every piece of the engine the dev has already built. It turns \"villain bound to citadel\" from a problem into a premise. It turns \"descend to retrieve the MacGuffin\" from a trope into a job. It turns the Guide from a monologue-delivering ghost into a nervous clerk with biscuits. It gives the player a villain whose voice is Gruber-Jack-Sheriff-GLaDOS and whose grief arrives through eleven-year-old household objects rather than through her own mouth. And it gives the player a memory, five years after they stop playing, of the two teacups on the wooden table — one for them, one steaming, untouched, for the person they replaced. That is a world that lives rent-free. Build that one.
