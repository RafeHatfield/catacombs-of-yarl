# Proposal III — *Vale, Interior*

*A rival-as-architect roguelike for Rafe's third option. Written to be chosen, not to balance a slate.*

---

## A) One-breath pitch

Twelve years after you walked out on the one peer who ever matched you, the Guild hands you a contract with an unreasonable fee and a known address: your former partner's estate in Sallowmarch, which she has spent a decade turning into an argument about the two of you — seven floors deep, descending backward through every year you shared and then into the years before you met her, with every room carved in the exact stone of a memory she remembers better than you do, and with her sealed at the bottom as the keystone you were hired to pull.

**Title: *Vale, Interior.*** Architectural trade-parlance for the inside of a commissioned house, and the villain's surname opened up for dissection. Cold tile on the storefront, warm grief underneath.

---

## B) The PC

**Kestrel Márn, 41.** Guild Retrieval Specialist, Class II. Twenty years in trade, the last eight mostly sober. Fluent in three regional dialects and one extinct one. Known in the Guild of Clean Work for refusing heroic work and accepting impossible work, which she insists is a meaningful distinction. Lives alone in a two-room walk-up above a dyer's shop in Ormery; pays rent on time; owns more books than furniture. Has not spoken Aris Vale's name in twelve years; has heard other people speak it and left the room.

**The voice.** First-person, Vlad-Taltos register — dry, pragmatic, professional, allergic to heroism, affectionate toward competence, drops into seriousness mid-sentence without flagging. Brust by way of Lynch, with a Corwin-of-Amber undertow when Kestrel starts telling the truth. The diary entries that surface between runs are **never addressed to the player**; they are addressed to a private reader Kestrel will never name, which the player gradually realizes is Aris.

**Diary entry samples** (eight, in chronological order):

> *On signing.* The retainer is three times standard. The retrieval fee is six times. The hazard clause has its own hazard clause. Nobody pays this much for a keepsake unless the keepsake is a person, and nobody pays this much for a person unless the person is already a problem. I signed anyway. I tell myself it was the money. I'm a fair liar, mostly to me.

> *After the second corridor.* The second corridor turned left where I expected it to. Then the third did. By the fifth, I understood the house wasn't guessing. The house had *notes.* Somewhere, twelve years ago, I'd complained about a dogleg hallway in a villa outside Orren, and she'd been listening the way she listened to everything: like she was going to need it later. Apparently later is now.

> *In the Library of Jobs Refused.* Every contract on the shelves is one I turned down. Names I forgot. One I'd forgotten I'd forgotten. She'd *kept* them. Not to blackmail — the seals are clean — but catalogued. Filed. A librarian's love. I sat on the floor for a count of thirty and told myself it was to check the floor for wards. It wasn't. The floor was fine. I wasn't.

> *Re: the pay structure.* Fifty percent on acceptance is customary. Eighty percent on acceptance means the client expects you to die. The remainder on delivery of an item from a woman who does not intend to let the item leave means the Guild clerk drafting this did the math and decided to insult me politely. I have marked the invoice. I will be alive to dispute it. Probably.

> *The Kitchen That Smells Right.* Rosemary and scorched butter. The exact wrong amount of salt — *her* wrong amount. I stood in the doorway and did not go in, because I know what I am, and what I am is a woman who would have eaten. She knew that too. That's why the plate is laid for one. The chair is pulled out. The bread is still warm. Twelve years. Still warm.

> *After the practice hall.* Our chalk marks are still on the pillar where we measured each other. She's taller in the marks than I remember. I'm shorter. Memory is a thief and a flatterer and I have been robbed by it for a decade. I left the chalk where it was. I didn't need to re-measure. I already know who's taller now.

> *The Childhood Bedroom.* The window's wrong. She was never in this room; this room is the one *before* her, the one she was building toward when I met her at sixteen. Which means she built it from stories. Which means she remembered my stories better than I did. I don't know what to do with that sentence. I'm writing it down so I stop having to think it.

> *Last page, somewhere.* I came for the thing she took. I am going to leave with the thing she is. I do not know, yet, which of those is the theft.

**Starting situation.** Osune Trell — the Guild's Sallowmarch-branch clerk and Kestrel's between-run anchor — hands her the contract across a heated counting-house desk one wet November afternoon. The client is listed as *A.V., Sallowmarch, private residence.* Osune does not meet Kestrel's eyes when she pushes it across. Kestrel signs it anyway. **Why she descends reluctantly:** not duty, not revenge, not mystery. She signs because the fee will let her stop. She will not stop.

## C) The villain

**Aris Vale, 43.** Former Guild Commissioning-Architect. Left the trade at 31 after the Hollowstair incident (which the Guild has not resolved and does not speak of). Trained alongside Kestrel from age 16 to 24 at the Guild's Sallowmarch house; taught her how to read a floor plan and how to leave a room without being seen leaving. The two of them were, briefly and never-formally, something more than partners. When Kestrel walked out at 29, she walked out of a standing argument they had been having for six years without naming it. Aris has not sent a letter in twelve years. She has sent, apparently, a house.

**The concentric layers of her villainy.**

*Outer layer — the Commission.* A named architect hired to deliver a named item. Clean paperwork. A Guild-sanctioned target. This is what Osune can see from her desk.

*Middle layer — the Argument.* Every room in the estate is a specific argument to a specific person about a specific shared memory. The villain is not defending a fortune or a god. She is defending a version of their history that Kestrel walked away from without hearing.

*Deep layer — the Keystone.* Aris has bound her own name into the load-bearing stone of the estate's seventh floor. She is literally holding the house up. She cannot leave. She did not intend to leave. The estate is a suicide, too, but a patient one — a suicide that will not fire until the right reader arrives to understand it.

*Deepest layer — the Hollowstair thing.* Aris did not leave the Guild by choice. She was asked to leave because of something she did that was either love or murder or both, and the Guild record is sealed, and the only other person who knows is Kestrel, who was there, and who has told herself for twelve years that she misremembers it. Her estate is, among other things, a correction of that misremembering.

**Five channels of kinetic villain presence through the dungeon.**

1. **The architecture itself.** Rooms that anticipate Kestrel's route. Doors that open for her specifically (and close on Guild-specialist ghosts who tried this before). Corridors measured to her stride. The building is not a trap; the building is *addressed*, the way a letter is addressed — sealed, stamped, specific.
2. **Written fragments.** Notes, contract clauses, marginalia in books Kestrel once owned, engraved dedications on gift-weapons. Aris's voice reaches Kestrel only in ink. She never speaks aloud until the final room.
3. **Bound servants, living and dead.** Tiber Hollen the majordomo; the Three Stews in the kitchen; the Notary buried in the arbitration-room wall; Mira Vale — Aris's dead younger sister — playing stones against an empty chair forever on the practice floor. Each of them knows Kestrel by name and each of them was told what to say if she arrived.
4. **The Commissioned Stone itself.** Because magic in this world runs on commissioned stone (see D), the walls respond to Aris's intent even in her absence. Some walls warm when Kestrel leans against them. One door refuses to open until Kestrel says the correct word, which she was told once, at a party, at twenty-two, and has since forgotten.
5. **The house's errata.** Places where the architectural bindings have folded into chimeras — a staircase that is also a centipede; a door that is also a mouth. These aren't intentional traps; they're *design-bugs*, and the journal fragments apologize for them specifically, which is somehow worse than if they had been traps.

**When and how grief is discoverable.** The grief is buried three layers deep and surfaced only sideways.

The surface grief is Aris's: she has built a house for a person who left. A player reads this in the first hour.

The middle grief is Kestrel's, which she does not know she carries: she remembers the Hollowstair incident wrong, and every floor she descends corrects one detail — the color of the door, the name the witness gave, the order of the deaths. A player reads this over the middle third of the game, through contradictions between journal fragments and Kestrel's own diary entries.

The deep grief is mutual and unnamed: at 22, Aris told Kestrel something at a party, and Kestrel laughed and changed the subject, because Kestrel was a specialist and specialists do not have conversations like that. Aris has spent twelve years building the room that laughter should have been. A player reads this only on a deep replay, when they find the party in floor four and the room it should have become on floor six and realize they are the same room.

**Five written-fragment voice samples, Gruber-Sheriff register.**

> *(First-room welcome.)*
> Kestrel — You will find the stair on your left. You always preferred the left. I have had it re-laid in the stone you liked at Orren; I remembered the grain. I have taken the liberty of assuming you came armed. If you did not, please see the table. Do not rush. The house will not hurry, and neither, I find, will I. — A.

> *(Contract-clause, arbitration room.)*
> **ARTICLE IV — OF RECIPROCAL TENANCY.** Whereas the Party of the First Part (hereafter *the Visitor*) has accepted commission to retrieve the Article; and whereas the Party of the Second Part (hereafter *the Architect*) has constructed the premises in anticipation thereof; it is stipulated, without ambiguity, that the Visitor has *already* accepted tenancy by the act of crossing the threshold. Eviction may be sought only by completion of the descent. Counter-eviction, by the Architect, is reserved at her sole discretion, and, as the Visitor may recall, her discretion was always the problem.

> *(Pinned to a gift-weapon.)*
> You will hate that I chose this for you. You will hate more that it fits your hand. I balanced it against the second knuckle, as you taught me — I did listen, even when you thought I was being charming at you. Use it on whatever you need to, Kes. Including, if you decide, the last door. I did not arm you to be kind. I armed you because I would like this to be a conversation, and you were always poor at conversations unsupported. — A.

> *(Margin of a book Kestrel once owned.)*
> You underlined this at nineteen and pretended, at twenty, you hadn't. I watched you do both. I have kept the book in the condition you left it, save for this line, which I could not help. You were *correct*, Kes. That is what I could never tell you without your flinching. You were correct, and I built the rest of my life around the minor adjustment of that fact.

> *(Last written words, Childhood Bedroom, before confrontation.)*
> Kestrel. If you are reading this, you are in the only room I could not design — I copied it from what you once described to me, at eighteen, the night you did not mean to tell me anything. I have loved this room for twelve years without permission. Come down when you are ready. I am the keystone; you know what that means for the house. I am sorry about the pay. It had to be unreasonable. You would not have come for less. — Aris.

## D) The world

**Setting.** Sallowmarch — a dampening border-district two hundred miles from Ormery, old-money estate country strung along a grey river, where the architects' guilds historically kept their summer practice-houses. The region's word for "afternoon" translates more accurately as *"the long wait."* Rafe should read one paragraph of Piranesi and score the whole district to that.

**The game takes place entirely inside one building: Vale Hall, Aris's inherited-and-extended estate.** No overworld. No city scenes. No second dungeon. Per the Obra Dinn / Disco Elysium / Edith Finch technique — **one container holds all the scales of meaning.** If the story cannot fit in this house, it does not exist.

### The ONE WEIRD IDEA

**The deeper you go, the younger the relationship gets.** The estate's seven floors map to reverse chronology of Kestrel and Aris's history, and the final floor is *before they met* — Aris's pre-Kestrel childhood self, carved from stories Kestrel once told her at nineteen and she remembered better than Kestrel did. Three layers stack on this descent-logic, each legible at a different zoom:

*At room-zoom (what this room is saying):* **every room is a memory re-carved in stone by someone who remembers it better than you.** The chairs are in the position from *that* night. The chalk marks are where they actually were, not where Kestrel thought. Aris is an unreliable narrator in Kestrel's favor — she has corrected Kestrel's misrememberings, and the corrections are sometimes unbearable.

*At descent-zoom (where I am in the history):* **Floor 1** is the present — Vale Hall's formal, professional public face. **Floor 2** is the late partnership, the year before Kestrel left. **Floor 3** is the working years, the jobs. **Floor 4** is the apprenticeship, ages 16–24, the practice hall and the shared dormitories. **Floor 5** is Aris at 15, the year before she met Kestrel. **Floor 6** is Aris's adolescence at home, Mira alive. **Floor 7** is the Childhood Bedroom, copied from a story Kestrel told once.

*At finale-zoom (the final twist):* **the bottom floor is still wet.** Mortar undried. The stones still warm. The final room is what Aris was finishing *the morning Kestrel arrived at the gate.* The descent is a race Kestrel did not know she was in and has, in fact, already lost.

Pitch line: *Every room is a memory she remembers better than you. The deeper you go, the younger you both get. The last room is the one she was building this morning.*

### The material magic runs on

**Commissioned stone.** Magic in this world is worked into masonry that has been *cut to a specific person's measure* — height, hand-span, stride, the lung-capacity of their first sigh. A wall built for you obeys you. A wall built about you, in your name, cut to your measure, argues with you. Commissioning-architects are the strongest mages in Sallowmarch by a comfortable margin, because any stone they cut for a named person is a spellbook with one permitted reader. **Aris was one of the three best working Commissioning-Architects of her generation.** The estate is her grimoire.

### The one specific economy

**The Favor-Debt, marked.** Guild specialists are paid in favors formally notched into a personal ledger-stone kept in the Guild vaults. Favors accrue interest. Death does not clear them. Specialists can borrow against their own ledger to pay for resurrection, training, bribes, lodgings — the ledger is career, credit, and corpse-price in one object. **Kestrel's ledger-stone is one of the oldest still in active service at Clean Work's Sallowmarch branch.** Osune has kept it oiled for twenty years.

### Factions, mapped to the existing systems

**The Guild of Clean Work** — the larger institutional frame. Specialists, clerks, arbiters, commissioning-architects. Both Kestrel and Aris are Guild. The Guild bills itself on hygiene ("clean work": no witnesses, no residue, paperwork filed) and is in practice a dense web of small corruptions around that word.

**Orcs — *The Vermeer Crew,* a mason-clan under a war-contract.** Aris hired them through a long-standing Guild instrument; they built the estate over eleven years and half the crew is still on-site finishing Floor 7. Three in-dungeon fits:
- Working masons encountered actively building, who respect paying professionals and can be bribed with coin or blueprints.
- **Foreman Tovek** — a single orc sergeant who remembers Kestrel's name from a joint Guild job and is the closest thing to a friendly in the building; he trades route info for small favors and will eventually warn her about Floor 6.
- The unpaid skeleton crew on the lowest floors, whose final-installment coin went into the Childhood Bedroom's window; they'll turn on Aris for cash.

**Undead — *The Previous Endeavors.*** Every prior Guild specialist Aris sent to test her own design. Aris is a specialist too; she stress-tested the estate against her own trade. Corpses propped in the rooms where they died, journals intact. Three in-dungeon fits:
- **Failed prior specialists,** named, with journals the PC can read — one of them dies in a way that reads uncomfortably like Kestrel's own recent diary entries.
- **The kept household** — Aris's late butler, her late tutor, the Three Stews — bound post-mortem to continue their duties because she couldn't bear to finish mourning. They serve tea. They fight only if the tea is refused.
- **Memory-ghosts** — imprints in intimate rooms, performing the original event *as Aris remembers it,* subtly wrong in Kestrel's favor.

**Clerics — *The Contract Arbitration Order.*** Lawyer-priests of the Guild, dispatched because Aris's final act will void a dozen binding contracts and the paperwork must be adjudicated on-site. Three in-dungeon fits:
- **Auditing clerics** — not allies, not enemies; their interest is paperwork, and they can *rewrite a room's rules mid-fight* by citing a binding clause. Turn-based combat loves this.
- **Brother Ellum** — Aris's private confessor, kept on retainer, now walking the house looking for someone to confess to because Aris sealed him out of Floor 7. Potential ally, potential burden, potential source on Hollowstair.
- **Clerics of the Hearth-God Aris renounced** — they've come to reclaim the estate's consecration and fight *the building itself,* making them situational allies on floors where a wall is actively trying to kill Kestrel.

**Monsters — *The Bindings.*** Aris bound elementals and spirits into load-bearing architectural elements: a wind in a corridor, a fire in a hearth, a water in the plumbing. Three in-dungeon fits:
- **Pinned bindings** that emerge as monsters when the stone is disturbed — mechanically these are the "break a wall, fight its spirit" encounters.
- **Pet-of-Aris, grown** — a creature Kestrel and Aris raised together during apprenticeship, left behind, fed, grown huge and lonely. Mid-boss. Recognizable. Recruitable on one specific path.
- **The errata** — stair-centipedes, door-mouths, the house's own design-bugs. Apologetically footnoted in the journal fragments. Beating them feels like beating Aris's work; sparing them, where possible, feels like honoring it.

### Osune Trell — the clerk-anchor

Fifty-something, widowed, runs Clean Work's Sallowmarch office out of a converted counting-house where the heating is theoretical. She balances Kestrel's favor-debt ledger with a copper-nib pen she has had since apprenticeship. Opinions about every client she does not share unless drunk. Calls Kestrel "Specialist Márn" in writing and "kid" in speech. Between runs, Kestrel returns to Osune's desk; Osune logs the run, adjusts the ledger, pours one cup of tea, tells Kestrel one thing she did not know about Aris. **Osune is the functional analog of Mord but with a specific texture Mord does not have: she knew Aris too, professionally, for years, and she has opinions she has been saving for twelve years, and she will tell them one per run in order of importance.**

## E) The MacGuffin — the Keystone Ledger

**What it is.** Kestrel's own ledger-stone. Every favor-debt she has incurred or collected across twenty years of Guild work is notched into a single basalt prism kept in the Guild vaults at Ormery — except, as Osune will eventually confess, it isn't in Ormery anymore. **Aris has spent twelve years quietly buying up Kestrel's ledger in pieces, through Guild shell-contracts, and has mortared the pieces into Vale Hall's load-bearing walls as commissioned stone.** The estate is held up, physically and magically, by Kestrel's own professional life.

**Why it's emotionally specific.** It is not a lost lover's letter (Vess) or a political artifact (Long Winter). It is **Kestrel's labor as Aris sees it** — every job catalogued, every refusal noted, every scar itemized. Aris has kept the worst of it close to her heart: the central keystone of Floor 7, the single stone the whole house rests on, is the one notched with **the single unclosed debt between the two of them,** from a job at twenty-three they never finished paying each other for. That's the piece Kestrel has been hired to retrieve.

**Why Aris can't give it to her.** By Guild law, a favor-debt stone requires Guild seal on both ends for transfer, and the paperwork for a multi-decade bulk-purchase of a specialist's active ledger is *infinite* — the Arbitration Order is still adjudicating year four. Practically: the ledger fragments are mortared into load-bearing stone. Removing them collapses the house, which kills Aris, who has bound her name into the keystone. **Aris cannot hand the ledger back without dying.** She has arranged for Kestrel to be the one who decides whether the debt is worth her life.

**How it's assemblable across runs.** Seven large ledger-fragments, one per floor, each representing a phase of Kestrel and Aris's shared history. A single successful run recovers one to three fragments depending on routing. The full set requires many runs, and each run exposes more inscriptions on already-recovered fragments as Kestrel learns to read them (run 1: "Hollowstair, age 26." Run 4: "Hollowstair, age 26, Aris took the fall."). The Keystone itself, on Floor 7, is recoverable only once the other six are in hand — and recovering it is the confrontation.

## F) Death-and-reset — the Second-Endeavor Clause

**Diegetic.** Every Guild Retrieval contract above a certain fee threshold includes a Second-Endeavor rider: if the specialist fails, the Guild will pay out a resurrection by drawing one year from the specialist's ledger-stone, filing the paperwork, and returning the specialist to the jobsite. This is in the small print of the contract Kestrel signed. Osune handles the paperwork.

**The twist that makes it devastating.** Each death costs one year off Kestrel's ledger-stone — the same ledger-stone Aris has bought up and mortared into Vale Hall. **So every time Kestrel dies, a fragment of the MacGuffin she is here to retrieve crumbles.** The longer she plays, the less ledger remains to recover. Osune, between runs, tells her what year was just filed off. ("Kid, that was 22 to 23. You took the Malvern job that year. You don't remember it anymore, do you.")

**Mechanical register.** The player has two resource-clocks running at once: the hit-point clock in the dungeon (immediate) and the ledger clock across the campaign (long-term). A player who dies a lot can still beat the game — but fewer ledger-fragments means fewer readable inscriptions means a shallower version of the truth. **Skill correlates with depth of story access.** The worst runs yield a different, thinner ending than the best ones.

**Why this is better than soul rotation.** Soul rotation is a mechanic. Second-Endeavor is a relationship: Osune is invested, Aris is invested (her house is literally eating what Kestrel came to recover), and the player *feels each death as an edit to the story they were piecing together.*

## G) Six-layer delivery architecture

1. **Ambient — the stone itself.** Wall-carvings, floor inlay, doorframe engravings. Commissioned stone *responds* to Kestrel's passage. A wall measured to her breath chills slightly when she exhales on it. No text, no pickups, no NPC — pure environmental texture. This layer carries the descent-logic: the floor's era is telegraphed by material (polished marble on Floor 1, oak beams on Floor 4, childhood-bedroom pine on Floor 7) and by commissioned-stone "temperature."

2. **Pickups — the paper layer.** Notes, contract clauses, marginalia, letters never sent, receipts, Guild invoices, Aris's personal journals, Kestrel's own old diary pages that Aris kept. **Alan Rickman register throughout for Aris; Vlad Taltos for Kestrel.** Full inventory across the game is approximately 120 pickups, of which a single run exposes 15–25. **The Ishiguro technique governs this layer: every pickup over-describes one physical object and never names the feeling about it.**

3. **Vaults — rooms as places.** The 14–18 handcrafted set-piece rooms, each a specific memory re-rendered (see section K for signature set). These are the **screenshot rooms**, deterministic in appearance but variable in content. They anchor each floor.

4. **Characters — servants and survivors.** Osune between runs; Tiber, the Three Stews, Mira's ghost, Edrin Calch, the Notary, Foreman Tovek, Brother Ellum, Lys within. Each has a one-question-per-encounter or one-confession-per-run structure that slowly surfaces the Hollowstair incident and the unnamed thing at the party.

5. **Antagonists — the bindings, the errata, the previous specialists.** Combat content doubles as narrative content: every monster, every chimera, every undead specialist has a name and a reason to be here. Defeating them yields specific lore drops, not generic loot. **No encounter in the dungeon is anonymous.**

6. **Meta — the ledger and the keystone.** The cross-run layer: what ledger-fragments you have assembled, what inscriptions you can now read, what Osune has told you this run, what years you have lost to Second-Endeavor deaths. The main menu of the game *is* Kestrel's ledger, displayed on Osune's desk, with notches visible and fragments slotted as you recover them.

## H) Replay deepening — concrete examples

**Run 1:** Kestrel reaches Floor 3. Recovers two ledger-fragments. Reads the first-floor notes as hostile. Reads the second-floor notes as menacing. Dies in the arbitration room to a cleric she argued with. Osune files 23–24 off her ledger.

**Run 3:** Kestrel reaches Floor 5. The first-floor notes, reread, now scan as *tender* — the player sees the same words Kestrel did on Run 1, but Kestrel's diary entries have shifted; she is reading them differently now. The Library of Jobs Refused, revisited, reveals a second hand in the margins — Aris's — annotating *why* she took each job Kestrel refused.

**Run 5:** Foreman Tovek, having seen Kestrel three runs in a row, breaks protocol and gives her a blueprint of Floor 6 that Aris didn't want her to have. Brother Ellum, drunk from a bottle he found in the Confessor's cell, tells Kestrel what he heard Aris say about Hollowstair.

**Run 7:** Kestrel reaches Floor 7 for the first time. Finds the Childhood Bedroom. Finds the final letter. Chooses one of three axis-positions for each of three axes (below). One of the endings fires.

**Run 10 (New Game Plus, same save):** Enters the house a second time. The architecture has shifted: Aris is dead or alive depending on the prior ending, and the rooms reflect it. The previous playthrough is *visible in the walls.* A player can scorch-earth New-Game-Plus to see the rarer endings.

**Throughout:** **every room's lore has at least three depth-tiers, revealed by revisit count.** A player who visits the Kitchen That Smells Right three times in three runs sees: (1) warm bread, plate laid; (2) the recipe card is in Kestrel's handwriting, and Aris's apron is hung where it always was; (3) the bread is baked fresh every morning because Tiber has instructions to, in case she comes back, in case she is hungry.

## I) Endings — three axes, stitched

**Axis 1 — What Kestrel does with the Keystone Ledger.**
*Take it* (retrieve, leave). *Sign it* (close the unclosed debt, keep both ledgers clean, change nothing else). *Break it* (refuse the debt, destroy what she was to Aris).

**Axis 2 — What happens to Aris.**
*Lives* (keystone extracted intact — requires all six prior fragments recovered and a specific dialogue path with Brother Ellum). *Dies* (keystone pulled from her body, house falls). *Becomes the house* (Aris refuses to come out; Kestrel seals her in and walks up; the estate continues, occupied).

**Axis 3 — What Kestrel does with the Guild.**
*Returns and bills them* (specialist behavior; Osune processes the paperwork; Kestrel buys the walk-up outright). *Returns and resigns* (hands Osune her ledger and walks away from Clean Work for the first time in twenty years). *Does not return* (vanishes into the Veruun; Osune files her missing; a letter arrives at the office six months later, postmarked from somewhere no one knows).

**Stitching — seven fully-rendered endings** (from 27 combinations; the others fail-safe to the nearest fully-written string):

1. ***The Clean Bill.*** Take it / Aris dies / Returns and bills them. The Guild-standard ending. Osune pours the tea. Kestrel sleeps through the next winter. The walk-up above the dyer is quiet. She does not dream of the kitchen.

2. ***Architect's Apology.*** Sign it / Aris lives / Returns and resigns. The hardest-to-earn good ending. The ledger is closed, the house stands, Aris is alive behind a door Kestrel will not knock on again; Kestrel hands Osune her copper pen and walks out.

3. ***The Letter from Sallowmarch.*** Break it / Aris becomes the house / Does not return. The cruelest ending: Kestrel destroys the debt, leaves Aris sealed in, vanishes. Six months later Osune receives a letter; the handwriting is Kestrel's; the postmark is Vale Hall.

4. ***Two Tenants.*** Sign it / Aris becomes the house / Does not return. Quiet, awful, true: both of them live in Vale Hall now. Neither comes out. The house answers when knocked on.

5. ***Clean Work, Done Properly.*** Take it / Aris dies / Returns and resigns. The specialist grows up. Osune breaks down once. Kestrel takes a job in Ormery as a dyer's assistant.

6. ***The Countersign.*** Sign it / Aris dies / Returns and bills them. The blackest comedy: Kestrel countersigns the ledger, *then* the house falls. She bills the Guild for the retrieval and the arbitration fee. Osune does not laugh.

7. ***Vale, Interior.*** Break it / Aris lives / Does not return. The titular ending. Kestrel walks out with nothing; Aris walks out with nothing; Vale Hall stands empty, the rooms wet-mortared and unused, a house about two people neither of whom lives in it. The final screen is a wide shot of the empty kitchen with the bread gone stale.

---

## J) Keep vs. replace from existing canon

**Keep.** The Guild-specialist PC archetype. The turn-based, continual-save, mobile-first structural commitments. The Oryx-tileset art direction (it is a *help* for this premise — it imposes a domestic-scaled pixel register that suits rooms-as-memories). The clerk-between-runs loop (reshape from Mord to Osune, but the beat is identical: paperwork as relational anchor). The four existing factions — orcs, undead, clerics, monsters — repurposed per section D. The consumable-magic, classless-fighter PC (Kestrel is a specialist, not a chosen one). Barter, signposts, murals, diaries, hidden rooms, contradictions, NPC interaction.

**Replace.** The "descend into evil, kill the boss, collect loot" framing. **Aggressively replace.** Combat in *Vale, Interior* is a conversation Aris forces when words fail; it is never the primary transaction. Also replace: any lore delivered through monologue. Also replace: any lore delivered through a "wise NPC who explains things" — Brother Ellum is a drunk with a grudge, Osune is a clerk with opinions, nobody knows the full story. Also replace: any notion of the PC as chosen. Kestrel is chosen only by Aris, which is the problem the game is about.

**Do not import from Vess or Long Winter.** No house-as-wife. No god-fragment in a crown. No tournament. No king. **The voice, the scale, and the conceit must be new.**

## K) Five signature images

1. **The Kitchen That Smells Right.** A still-warm loaf of bread on a scrubbed table, one chair pulled out, a recipe card in Kestrel's handwriting pinned above the stove. Light comes through a rain-streaked window. A single wet footprint — not Kestrel's — by the pantry door. Store-page hero shot.

2. **The Library of Jobs Refused.** A narrow, very long hall, scroll-racks running to a vanishing point, one candle per contract — hundreds. Kestrel sitting on the floor halfway down, knees to chest, a single scroll open on her lap. The candles are *all* lit. Nobody has lit them for this visit.

Every job Aris did alone after Kestrel left marked in iron pins. The iron vastly outnumbers the copper. A single red pin in the corner, stuck in Ormery, on the exact street where Kestrel lives now.

## L) Scope and sequencing — solo dev, 6–12 months, MVP path

**The scope conceit.** Seven floors. Each floor is 1–2 handcrafted memory set-pieces + 4–6 procgen rooms themed to the floor's era + 1 transition. Total handcrafted rooms: **14–18**. This is tractable for a solo dev leveraging Oryx tiles because the handcrafted rooms are set-dressing over the same tile budget, not bespoke art.

**Months 1–2 — Vertical slice.** Floor 1 playable end-to-end. Second-Endeavor death-loop wired. Osune-between-run scene functional. One memory set-piece (**The Kitchen That Smells Right**), one procgen floor layout, one ledger-fragment assembly. One ending path stubbed. **Ship this internally to play-testers.** This is already a game.

**Months 3–4 — Floors 2–4 (partnership years).** The jobs era. Library of Jobs Refused, Map Room, practice hall set-pieces. Mid-boss: Pet-of-Aris, grown. Foreman Tovek introduced. First major journal-fragment payload. Contract-clause arbitration mechanic implemented.

**Months 5–6 — Floors 5–6 (pre-Kestrel Aris).** Mira Vale's ghost. The greenhouse. The oubliette with one occupant. Brother Ellum introduced; Hollowstair reveal mid-layered. Second mid-boss: the house's errata (a stair-centipede).

**Months 7–8 — Floor 7 and the three confrontations.** The Childhood Bedroom set-piece. Three final-room variants (Unfinished Room, Arbitration Chamber, Childhood Bedroom proper) branching on player prior choices. All seven written endings written, recorded, implemented.

**Months 9–10 — Replay deepening, content tier-2 and tier-3.** Second and third lore tiers for every handcrafted room. New-Game-Plus architectural shifts based on prior ending. Additional secondary NPCs (Edrin Calch, the Notary, Lys).

**Months 11–12 — Polish, audio, balance, release.** Audio direction critical: **commissioned stone wants a stonework ambient layer** — chisel hits, settling mortar, distant hammering — and **Aris's written voice should be delivered in silent text; no voice acting.** The only spoken audio in the game is Osune's between-run line-readings and the final room's confrontation.

**MVP ship condition.** If the solo dev hits month 8 and has to ship, Floors 1–4 plus one compressed final-floor confrontation is a complete playable story. The game gets worse with cuts but does not break.

**Technical risk.** The three genuinely new systems are: (a) the ledger-stone as meta-save layer, (b) the contract-clause combat modifier system for arbitration-room encounters, (c) the commissioned-stone "personalized wall response" telegraphing. Everything else uses existing combat, existing faction balance, existing pickup/diary infrastructure. **The ledger-stone system is the biggest technical risk and should be spiked in month 1.**

## How this compares to *The Vess House* and *The Long Winter*

**The three proposals form a clean emotional triangle, not a line.** Vess is *grief.* Long Winter is *legacy.* Vale is *the one about being known too well by someone you were wrong about.* None of the three is merely a warmer or cooler version of another; each names a different human catastrophe.

**On tone.** Vess is intimate-domestic, claustrophobic, Annihilation-by-Piranesi. Long Winter is mythic-political, nation-stakes, Hyperion-by-Locke-Lamora. Vale is intimate-professional, Prestige-by-Piranesi, Ishiguro-voiced — it shares Vess's scale of consequence (one person, one building) while sharing Long Winter's structural rigor (a constructed adversary with concentric layers). It does not share Vess's *domesticity* (nobody is married here; the intimacy is peer-professional, which is a register neither prior proposal occupies) and it does not share Long Winter's *world-shaking*. **The stakes never extend beyond two people and a building, but the emotional weight is absolute.**

**On asset leverage.** Vale is the most natural fit for Rafe's existing factions of the three. Orcs-as-masons is a genuinely fresh use of the sprite set that neither prior proposal offers. Undead-as-previous-specialists re-uses the skeleton/zombie palette with specific narrative weight per instance. Clerics-as-arbiters gives Rafe a *combat mechanic* (contract-clause rules rewriting) that the balancing work he's already done can absorb directly. Monsters-as-bindings explains why monsters appear in what is, ostensibly, a private estate — the house is full of pinned things. **Vale demands no new asset class and rationalizes every existing one.** Long Winter, by contrast, has to contort orcs into "Crown Guard" and clerics into "Coronation Order," which works but is visibly retrofitted. Vess has less asset leverage at all — a house of grief does not obviously contain orc raiders.

**On risk profile.** Vale's biggest risk is **novelty overshoot** — the premise is more conceptually singular than Long Winter, which is the dev's explicit ask, but unfamiliar premises cost discoverability on mobile storefronts. "Dungeon built by your ex-partner as an argument" is a great pitch in a pitch meeting and a hard pitch in a 160-character app-store description. Mitigation: *Vale, Interior* reads as genre-legible (architectural horror? intimate mystery?) without giving the premise away. Secondary risk: **the Ishiguro register is easy to get wrong.** If Kestrel's diary entries drift into melodrama, the whole thing collapses into Saw-with-ex-girlfriends. The writing standard is absolute and non-negotiable.

**On shippability.** Vale is **the most shippable of the three on the 6-month timeline** — one building, seven floors, handcrafted set-pieces at a 14–18 room budget, no overworld, no second location, no political faction-system to balance. Vess is similarly tight in scope but leans harder on environmental storytelling that may demand bespoke art. Long Winter is the most ambitious (nation-scale factions, multiple locations implied, a tournament structure) and the hardest to ship solo in a year. **If Rafe has to ship by month 8 with cuts, Vale degrades most gracefully** — the middle floors can compress without the story breaking.

**On premise originality.** The dev's honest critique of Long Winter — *"badass but not as unique as Vess"* — is right. The Hunger-Games / royal-deathmatch cluster drags Long Winter closer to genre center-of-mass than Vess. Vale pushes back toward Vess-level singularity without repeating Vess's technique: *Vess's weird idea is "descend into a dead person's biography"; Vale's weird idea is "descend into a living rival's biography, which is also an argument she is actively finishing."* The nearest roguelike comparable to Vale is Inscryption (authored gamemaster), the nearest AAA is Hades 2 (parent-overwrites-architecture), the nearest literary is the Prestige-by-Piranesi-by-Amadeus, and *no existing work occupies the intersection.* Long Winter's nearest comparables are a much more populated neighborhood.

**Verdict.** If Rafe wants the safest ship, pick Vess. If he wants the biggest swing at genre, pick Long Winter. If he wants **the game that will get screenshotted and argued about on forums for five years**, pick *Vale, Interior.* It is less grandiose and more personal than Long Winter, structurally distinct from Vess, and it leverages the factions he's already balanced more naturally than either. It is the hardest to write and the easiest to build. It is also the one that, if it lands, is his.
