# The Long Rotation — Solo Dev Edition

*The original story, rescued, and scoped to something you can actually ship alone*

---

## What I'm cutting, and why

The previous version treated the rescue as a mandate to preserve and amplify every strong element from your original into full architectural weight. That was wrong for your reality. Let me name what I'm pulling back, so you know what trade-offs I'm making:

**Cut: the "one soul across 200 lives" metaprogression text.** A solo dev cannot write recovered-memory flavor for every unlock and every item and sustain voice quality. The soul-rotation conceit is preserved, but it's handled by **four or five specific scripted moments** across the game, not by a pervasive authored layer. You get the emotional payoff of "you've been here before" without needing to write 50,000 words to sustain it.

**Cut: Myrren's distributed consciousness.** The idea that a dead partner's soul is split across the Guide, the PC, and the villain is a really cool idea I got excited about. It's also a solo-dev trap — the logic has to be invisible to players until it reveals, which means you have to write *two* versions of every Myrren scene (pre-reveal and post-reveal). That's a doubling of workload for a clever twist. Cut it. Myrren is dead. She speaks in recovered letters. That's enough.

**Cut: seven named Vigil members with full arcs.** Simplified to **three** — the leader, the doubter, and the child victim. Each has a reconstructible fate from found correspondence, but you're writing three character dossiers, not seven.

**Cut: six endings with distinct final-image cinematics.** Simplified to **four endings** that share structural anchors but differ meaningfully in outcome. A solo dev can write four epilogues well. Six is where quality starts to degrade.

**Cut: the "common word you've been saying since floor one" true-name reveal.** Elegant in theory, a nightmare to execute — every opening dialogue line has to be reread after the ending and still make sense. Replaced with a simpler, also-satisfying version: **the true name is a word you learn from a specific journal fragment in a specific room, and the game tells you you've learned it.** Less clever, just as emotional, massively easier to ship.

What remains is still the rescue. Every problem I diagnosed with the original is still fixed. You still get the dragon-widower, the citadel-as-body, the merged villain, the soul-rotation frame, the Guide, the true name, and morally distinct endings. You just don't get the AAA version of each.

## A) One-breath pitch

*The dragon at the bottom of the stairs has been cursed for four hundred years into a mortal body, bound to the prison the cultists built around him — which is literally his own calcified body, because that's what they were doing when the ritual went wrong. His partner is dead. He has spent four centuries cycling souls through bodies, sending them down to retrieve her heart from the deepest vault. You are the latest. You have been here before. You don't remember yet.*

Same dragon-widower premise. Same soul rotation. Same Ruby Heart at the bottom. What changes: the citadel is his body (specificity), his partner Myrren has a profession and letters that make her a real person (fix the two-villain problem), and the endings are four genuinely different outcomes rather than three difficulty tiers.

---

## B) The PC

You are the latest soul. The previous twelve or fifteen (the game doesn't specify exactly; you find evidence that the number is at least in the dozens) wore different bodies, had different lives. You have been a cartographer, a deserter, a baker's daughter, a sellsword, others you'll never know. This body is new. The soul is not.

The player learns this in three scripted revelations:

**Revelation 1 (floor 3):** You enter a room you have never been in and your hand finds a hidden latch you couldn't possibly know. A flavor line: *You reach for it without thinking. The knowledge is older than this body.*

**Revelation 2 (floor 10):** The Guide, on meeting you, says *"Oh — it's you again. New skin. Good one this time."*

**Revelation 3 (floor 17):** In a Memory-Reliquary room, you find a small shelf of *objects you have carried in previous lives* — a ring, a journal, a child's toy. You don't remember them, but the game identifies them as yours. This is the emotional beat that lands "you've been here many times."

That's the entire soul-rotation payload. Three scripted moments, no pervasive text layer. You feel the weight without requiring the dev to sustain it constantly.

**Voice.** The PC has first-person diary entries between floors, in the Vlad Taltos register you've been building toward. Short, wry, practical. Roughly 25–30 entries for the whole game — one per floor or so — each 50–80 words. About 2,000 words of PC voice total.

Sample entries:

> *Floor 3.* Found a latch I shouldn't have known about. My hand knew. My head did not. I have decided to stop asking my hand questions, because I suspect the answers will ruin my week.

> *Floor 10.* The ghost said "new skin." I did not ask her what she meant. I am going to ask her on floor eleven, if she's still there. I am also going to stop being surprised about things, as a general policy, because surprise is becoming tiring.

> *Floor 17.* There is a shelf down here with a ring on it. A plain iron band. When I picked it up the shopkeeper-ghost at the counter — there is a shopkeeper-ghost; I have made my peace with this — she said "you left that here last time, dear." I put the ring on. It fits. Of course it does. I'm keeping it. The ghost did not seem to mind.

The PC's name can be player-generated or you can pick one and commit. For simplicity and voice consistency, I'd commit. Call her **Sera Vann**. Sellsword's daughter. Came down for the money. Not noble. Not chosen. Owes her mother three months of back-rent.

## C) The villain, as one person

**Ysselen Hrask**, once called the Colder, now just called Ysselen by anyone who's been down here long enough to know. Dragon cursed into mortal form by a ritual gone wrong four hundred years ago. Bound to the citadel because the citadel is him. Grieving. Bored. Tired. Dangerous because he can't leave and no one who comes down can go home.

**The merge of the two villains.** Myrren is dead and stays dead. You never meet her. You meet her *correspondence* — letters in Ysselen's desk drawers that she wrote to him in the weeks before the ritual, drafts of a book they were writing together, margin notes in his books where she corrected his spelling. She is present as *evidence of a marriage*. That's enough. You don't need a ghost or a hidden consciousness. A well-written letter hits harder than either.

**Ysselen as present villain.** He has four channels through the game, but they're all lightweight:

1. **Broadcast dialogue.** He speaks into the citadel at scripted moments — five or six per run. Short lines. Under twenty words each.
2. **Written marginalia.** About 15 pieces of Ysselen's writing scattered through the dungeon: a diary he keeps in his throne chamber that you find early and read updates from when you revisit, old pre-curse letters to Myrren, drafts of a treatise he's been writing for centuries.
3. **The clock.** A mechanical clock on a plinth in his throne chamber. He takes it apart and puts it back together compulsively. When you visit the chamber, the clock is in a different state of assembly. That's the whole mechanic. It's just set dressing. But it *characterizes* him on every visit.
4. **The final encounter.** One scripted audience on floor 25, four variants based on your ending choice.

Total Ysselen writing budget: maybe 3,000 words. That's it. A villain that specific doesn't need more.

**Voice samples, tight:**

> *(Broadcast, after you kill a guard.)*
> *"That was Tovin. He had a wife in the upper township. I'll write to her. I'll say he died well. He did not. But she'll prefer the letter my way."*

> *(Marginalia, in a book of poetry Myrren gave him.)*
> *She underlined this line. I have never liked it. I keep the book because she underlined it, not because I like it, and I will not pretend otherwise, to her memory or to myself.*

> *(Diary entry, updated when you reach floor 15.)*
> *Another one on fifteen. New body, old soul. I recognize her walk. She has tripped on the same flagstone three times across three lives. I should move it. I will not. Let her trip. It is, I find, the closest thing I have to company.*

> *(Pre-curse letter to Myrren, found in his desk.)*
> *Myrren — the clock is done. I built it for the hours we keep apart, which I find I cannot tolerate, and since you will not move your work north to me, I am sending time itself to you. Wind it on Sundays. Think of me when it chimes. — Y.*

> *(Final audience, ending VI, the only line that matters.)*
> *"You spoke my name. No one has spoken my name since she died. Please say it again."*

That's Ysselen. Alan Rickman, specific, grieving, bound, witty when he has energy, tired when he doesn't. Total dialogue fits in a single medium-length document.

## D) The world

The citadel is called **Suth**. It is Ysselen's calcified body. The Vigil of Suth — three named surgeon-theologians, not seven — tried four hundred years ago to bind the dragons' soul-transfer ritual and failed catastrophically. They died. He fused to the stone around them. The ritual is still running on autopilot, rotating souls through bodies.

**The one weird idea, kept from the big version:** the citadel is him. Floors are anatomy. The Scale-Wards on floors 1–5 are outer plates. The Bellows on 6–10 are his lungs (doors that open and close on a 90-second breath cycle — great mobile-sized puzzle mechanic). The Spinework on 11–15 is his spine, with the Vigil's library cantilevered off his vertebrae. The Silent Ministries on 16–20 are his organs, retrofitted as the Vigil's living quarters. The Cathedra on 21–24 is his skull. Floor 25 is his tongue, called the Unfed Mouth.

**This is the one ambitious element I'm keeping intact** because it's what makes the rescue *the rescue*. The specificity of "the dungeon is his body" is what lifts this above generic fantasy. Without it, you're building the original again. With it, the world becomes unmistakable.

**The material magic runs on.** Keep it simple — **bond-script**, the shared handwriting Ysselen and Myrren wrote in. It's the only magical system in the game. Items in bond-script are magical. Spells are written in bond-script. Scrolls are letters. The true name, when you find it, is written in bond-script. This gives you one visual signature (a distinctive squiggly script style) that you can apply to every magical item, and it binds Myrren to every magical thing you pick up.

**Factions, leveraging your existing engine.** Same as the other proposals, minimal reskin:

- **Orcs — The Guard Remnant.** Original citadel garrison, now feral, patrolling the upper floors. No special story role — these are just monsters. You're saving the complexity budget for other characters.
- **Undead — The Vigil Dead.** The three named cultists plus their staff, animated by the botched ritual. Most are unnamed enemies. Three are named NPCs with dialogue.
- **Clerics — The Suturers.** The Vigil's spiritual arm, dead and reanimated. Floor 16 (liver-refectory) has a small group of them still performing their old rites. Can be fought or conversed with.
- **Monsters — Ysselen's Antibodies.** His body's immune responses, active on deeper floors. Just monsters. No story weight.

**Names (short list, committed):**
- **Sera Vann** — PC
- **Ysselen Hrask** — villain
- **Myrren** — dead partner (only named, never seen)
- **The Guide** — previous PC, no given name; she can't remember it (which you learn late and which lands as one of the saddest moments)
- **Vorel Ysethryn** — First Warden of the Vigil, miscounted the ritual
- **Ossa Velven** — the Linguist, the doubter
- **Wren Suth** — the child victim, age 13, the prototype

That's seven total named characters for the whole game. Shippable.

**Title: The Long Rotation.** Or if you want something shorter and punchier, **Suth**. I'd use The Long Rotation for the store page and Suth for the in-game title card.

## E) The MacGuffin

**Myrren's Ruby Heart, locked in the deepest vault.** Simplified from the big version's "it's actually inside Ysselen" twist, which was cool but added a layer of complexity your endings would need to explain.

**Here:** the heart is exactly where the original said it was — a vault on floor 25. You descend to retrieve it. The twist isn't about location; it's about **what you do with it once you have it.** The ending branches all happen in the Unfed Mouth after the heart is in your hand.

This is dramatically simpler to build and it still gives you the same satisfying climb-to-the-bottom loop. No hidden gizzard twist. No revelation inversions. Just: the thing is at the bottom, you get there, you decide.

## F) Death-and-reset

**You die, you wake up in the Unfed Mouth, Ysselen greets you.** Same mechanism as the big version, but stripped down.

Ysselen's greeting dialogue has maybe six scripted variants that cycle by death count and progress state. No need for infinite variation — players will see three or four across a completed playthrough. That's enough.

Sample greetings:

> *Death 1.* *"Oh. You again. New body, same soul. Let's see how this one lasts."*

> *Death 5.* *"You are getting better at floor seven. I should have moved the breath-valve. I will not. I like this version of you."*

> *Death, after you've been to floor 20.* *"You were so close. You always are. Come on. Try again."*

> *Death, after you've carved your name on the Tally Wall.* *"Forty-four. You are, statistically, the most persistent. I should find that depressing. I don't."*

The Tally Wall on floor 19 is the only real metaprogression surface — a wall with marks for every rotation. You can carve your name. The marks increment. No more complex than that.

## G) Delivery architecture — scoped to solo-dev reality

Four layers, deliberately lean:

**Layer 1 — Ysselen's broadcasts and marginalia.** About 20 pieces total. Your highest-quality writing load. Budget: two weeks of focused writing.

**Layer 2 — The Guide's appearances.** She shows up at three named rooms (not fixed depths): the cartographer's loft on floor 12, the Name-Chapel on floor 23, her own body on floor 17. Each encounter is three or four exchanges. About 500 words total.

**Layer 3 — Letters and journals.** Myrren's letters (about 8), Vigil correspondence (about 10 across the three named cultists), pre-curse Ysselen's treatise-in-progress (3 fragments). About 3,000 words. Half of it can be environmental set-dressing you can tap to read, half is pickup-based.

**Layer 4 — Sera's diary.** 25–30 entries, 50–80 words each. About 2,000 words.

**Total writing budget: about 8,500 words.** That's a long short story. Achievable in 6–8 weeks of focused writing with revision passes. Compare to Vess House at 15,000 and Vale at 16,000. **This is the lightest-writing version of the three "deep" games in your portfolio.**

---

## H) Replay — lean version

The soul-rotation framing does most of the replay work for free. You die, you wake up, Ysselen comments. That's enough to make each run feel connected.

Concrete replay hooks, minimal:

- **The Tally Wall.** Marks accumulate. Cheap. Satisfying.
- **Ysselen's throne-chamber clock.** Different configuration each visit. One line of flavor per state. About eight states. Once you've seen all eight, you've seen it — that's fine.
- **Rotation count affects Guide dialogue tier.** Three tiers (runs 1–5, 6–15, 16+). Her voice degrades across tiers. Tier 3 is where she calls you by the name she can't quite remember, which is your name from a previous life. Devastating, cheap to implement.

No flag-based reactive dialogue system at scale. No memory-line text on every unlock. The lean version accepts that replay depth is a bonus, not a pillar.

## I) Endings — four, differentiated by consequence

Floor 25, Unfed Mouth, you have the heart. Four choices. Each fires an epilogue of 400–600 words.

**Ending I — Walk Away.** Take the heart, leave. Ysselen rages as you climb back up. No boss fight. The citadel's antibodies fight you on the way out. You succeed or you die trying. Ending epilogue: you sell the heart to a collector, pay your mother's rent, and never sleep the same again.

**Ending II — Give It Back.** Hand the heart to Ysselen. He takes it. It shatters in his claws — the soul-transfer mechanism can't reconstitute her. He collapses. He thanks you, quietly, as he dies. The curse breaks. The citadel falls. You walk out. Souls are freed without memory.

**Ending III — Destroy It.** Shatter the heart without speaking his name. The curse breaks violently. Ysselen transforms, grief-mad, into a partial dragon form. Boss fight — winnable but hard. You win. Souls are freed, but Ysselen is dead in a rage, not at peace. You leave changed.

**Ending IV — Speak His Name.** This is the redemptive ending. You have to find his true name first — it's in Ossa Velven's chamber on floor 23, in the one candidate name she didn't cross out, which is hidden behind a door you need to have found a key for earlier. Speak his name at the heart. The curse breaks cleanly. Ysselen dies with her name on his lips. Souls are freed with memory intact. You remember your previous lives. You live with that.

Four endings. Four genuinely different outcomes. No same-fight-at-different-difficulty problem. Three are cutscene endings; one is a boss fight; one requires a specific item; none are just reskins of each other.

**Why four and not six:** you can write four endings *well* in a solo dev timeline. Six is where quality starts degrading.

## J) What this keeps from your original — every single thing you loved

- **Soul rotation conceit:** kept, delivered in three scripted revelations and Ysselen's between-run greetings. ✓
- **Guide as previous PC:** kept, appears at three named rooms, voice degrades across tiers. ✓
- **Villain's pain being real while he still betrays you:** kept — Ysselen is a grieving widower and he still kills every soul who comes down. ✓
- **True-name mechanic:** kept, simplified — name is findable in Ossa's chamber, spoken at the heart. ✓
- **Morally distinct endings:** kept, rebuilt as four genuinely different outcomes. ✓

Plus the rescue-added wins: the citadel is his body (the single biggest specificity upgrade from the original), the Vigil are three specific people, Myrren exists as letters rather than as abstract backstory, Ysselen has a clock.

## K) Signature images — four, not five

Four moments that will live in player memory:

1. **The clock on the plinth.** Ysselen's throne chamber, the mechanical clock in mid-disassembly on your first visit, half-reassembled on your second, fully intact on your third. Speaks volumes about him without one line of dialogue.

2. **The Tally Wall.** Floor 19. Forty-two marks at game start. A carving tool on the floor. You become mark forty-four.

3. **The ring on the shelf.** Floor 17, the Memory-Reliquary. The plain iron ring that doesn't fit on any finger but yours because it's already yours. The moment the game makes you *feel* the rotation instead of telling you about it.

4. **Ysselen, asking.** Floor 25, Ending IV. You speak his name. He lifts his head and says *"Please say it again."* Cut to white.

## L) Scope and sequencing

**Real scope, honestly.**

- **Writing:** 8,500 words. 6–8 weeks focused.
- **Handcrafted content:** Three named-NPC dialogue trees, about 15 scripted rooms (throne chamber, Memory-Reliquary, Tally Wall room, Name-Chapel, the three Vigil members' quarters, cartographer's loft, Guide's body chamber, plus seven anatomical floor-identity rooms), four ending epilogues. Maybe 3–4 months of content work.
- **Mechanical systems:** Tally Wall persistence (trivial), breath-cycle door puzzle (one system, reused on Bellows floors), Ysselen's clock state tracking (trivial), Guide voice-tier progression (trivial), true-name-speaking mechanic in final room (trivial). Maybe 2–3 weeks of engineering beyond your existing engine.

**Total realistic solo-dev timeline: 5–6 months** after Vess House ships. This is now comparable in scope to Vale — ambitious but *shippable*. Not a 4-year AAA project.

**Where this sits in your portfolio.**

Originally I said this should be the fourth release. I'm standing by that, but for different reasons now:

- **Drown's Gate** ships first. Engine shakedown, comedy, fast to market.
- **Vale, Interior** ships second. Tight mood piece, proves tonal range.
- **Vess House** ships third. Environmental villain, proves atmospheric commitment.
- **The Long Rotation** ships fourth. *Because by then you've written 30,000+ words of game fiction in three distinct registers and you know your own voice.* Ysselen specifically requires a confident writer. The three prior releases give you that confidence.

But the **simplified version fits comfortably in solo-dev scope**. It's no longer a capital-P Project. It's a game you can build in six months with a polished engine and three shipped titles of writing experience behind you.

## Honest note

I overbuilt the first rescue because I was excited about the concept of the rescue itself — can I save this? — and I let the problem of preservation override the problem of scope. That was my mistake, and your pushback caught it.

This version gives up the most elegant parts of the big version — the heart-inside-the-villain twist, the distributed consciousness, the word-you've-been-saying true name — and keeps what actually matters: the specificity that separates this story from generic fantasy, the merged villain that gives you one emotional center, and a structure that lets you tell your story without requiring a novelist's writing load.

**Your original story was never broken.** It was unfinished. This is what a finished version looks like at solo-dev scope. Four endings instead of six, three named cultists instead of seven, one villain instead of two, a dragon with a clock and a drawer full of letters, and a citadel that happens to be his body.

You can build this. When you're ready.
