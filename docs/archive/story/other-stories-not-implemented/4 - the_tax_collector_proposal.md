# Proposal IV — *The Tax Collector of Drown's Gate*

*A casual-friendly roguelike for Rafe's engine-shakedown release. Written to be played in fifteen-minute bites and still leave a thumbprint.*

---

## Strategic framing

This proposal solves a different problem than the first three. Vess, Long Winter, and Vale are all stories designed to *reward* deep attention. They fail at fifteen-minute bite-play because their payoff structure needs sustained reading. **A casual-first release needs the opposite: a premise the player understands in thirty seconds, a loop that is satisfying with zero story engagement, and a layer of texture that rewards the players who happen to notice it without punishing the players who don't.**

The move is a well-worn premise with **one confident twist** that colors everything downstream, plus a Pratchett-level light touch on voice. Familiar enough that a player picking this up at a bus stop knows exactly what they're doing; textured enough that the same player on their fifth run goes *"wait, what?"*

The twist for proposal #4: **you're not a hero. You're a bureaucrat.** Specifically, you are a Tax Assessor dispatched by a failing kingdom to audit a flooded, monster-infested, half-collapsed border fort that owes the Crown eleven years of back-taxes. The monsters are not a mystical evil to be defeated; they are **residents**, many of whom are also behind on their taxes, and some of whom are helpful if you read the paperwork right. You descend the fort floor-by-floor with a clipboard, a sword (standard issue, Grade 3, non-negotiable hazard pay), and the polite, unkillable stubbornness of a civil servant who needs three signatures before close of business.

The premise is *Shattered Pixel Dungeon* meets *Going Postal*. Every player knows how to play it in the first thirty seconds: go down, fight things, find loot, descend. Every player who reads a single item description laughs. Every player who reads three of them is suddenly invested in the bureaucracy.

---

## A) One-breath pitch

*You are a junior Tax Assessor in a collapsing kingdom, sent to audit Fort Drown — which owes eleven years of back-taxes, is technically still a royal asset, and is currently 80% underwater and 100% full of monsters. Your mandate is to descend, catalogue everything, collect what you can, and file a final report. The fort has its own opinions about this.*

---

## B) The PC — Assessor Wel Torran

**Wel Torran, age 28.** Junior Assessor, Royal Ministry of Taxes and Tributes, Class D (Provincial). Seven years with the Ministry. Has a good stamp collection. Owns a cat (Ambrose) left with a neighbor. Not a fighter in any meaningful sense — competent with a blade the way a clerk is competent with a letter opener, which is to say yes, but you don't want to watch. Thin. Polite. Wears spectacles with a crack in the left lens that the Ministry has refused to reimburse for eight months.

Wel did not volunteer for the Drown's Gate assignment. The assignment arrived in Wel's pigeonhole on a Monday morning, cosigned by three department heads, none of whom Wel has ever met. The Ministry does this when they are out of people willing to go. Wel went because the alternative was to refuse, which would have meant a disciplinary hearing, which would have meant the chance of losing Assessor status entirely, which would have meant losing the pension that Wel's mother depends on. *This is a career story.* Wel is here because Wel was assigned.

**The voice.** First-person, Vlad Taltos by way of Moist von Lipwig by way of an overworked Tom Hanks character. Wry but not bitter. Observant of the absurd but not cynical. Uses the word "regrettably" without irony. Calls monsters "residents" in written reports. Has a running internal joke with themselves about what the expense form for this trip will look like.

**Diary entry samples:**

> *Day 1.* Arrived Drown's Gate morning. Heavy rain. Outer gatehouse collapsed c. 1614, per the sergeant's notes, which are the only Ministry records extant. Sergeant was a goblin. Goblin was holding a ledger. Goblin asked if I was here about the drainage levy. I am not here about the drainage levy. I am here about eleven years of everything, but I did not say that. I said I was here to assess. The goblin asked if I wanted tea. I said yes. The tea was fine.

> *Day 2.* Descended to Floor 2. Encountered what the Ministry field guide calls "Lesser Ghoul (Undead, Class 4, Hostile)." The ghoul was named Petra. Petra was not, in fact, hostile — Petra was trying to return a library book. The book was two hundred years overdue. I issued Petra a Form R-17 (Courtesy Reminder) and accepted the book for return. The book was *Principles of Basic Hydraulics*, which I did not expect to be useful and which, four hours later, was useful.

> *Day 3.* Item lost: spectacles (Grade 2, Ministry-issue, cracked). Item found: spectacles (Grade 1, silver-chased, engraved in a language I do not recognize, visibly superior to my previous pair). Expense form consequences: uncertain. I am wearing them. They work. I will sort out the paperwork retroactively, which is against policy and which I have decided is acceptable under section 14c of the Emergency Assessment Powers.

> *Day 5.* Met an orc today who is running a small tea shop on Floor 4. Her name is Morrag. She has been running the tea shop for nine years. She is not on any Ministry rolls. She has been, in the most literal possible sense, *not paying taxes*. She served me tea while I explained this. The tea was very good. I have filed her as a new business registration effective backdated to whenever she started. The rate was reasonable. I accepted a discount on future tea in lieu of a setup fee. I am not certain this was regulation. I am certain it was correct.

> *Day 8.* Did a bad thing today, in that I killed a thing. It was going to kill me. The Ministry expense form has a box for this. The box is labeled "Category 4 Engagement (Self-Defense)." I ticked it. I will not write about this further. Forms are better.

> *Day 12.* Found a room on Floor 6 that contains eleven thousand coins arranged in obsessively neat stacks. There is a skeleton sitting at the desk. The skeleton has a quill. The skeleton's books are open and the ledger is *beautiful* — every entry balanced, dated, cross-referenced, cited. I sat with the skeleton for a while. The skeleton is, in a meaningful sense, one of the best accountants I have ever encountered. I have not decided what to do about this.

> *Day 15.* Report to the Crown pending. I am, it turns out, going to have opinions in it.

**Item flavor in Wel's voice:**

> *Shortsword, Ministry-issued, Grade 3.* Hazard pay is calculated based on the assumption that I will, on average, use this twice per assignment. I am currently averaging four uses per day.

> *Potion, unlabeled, blue.* Smells like peppermint and apology. I do not know what it does. The regulations on drinking unknown potions during Ministry work are ambiguous. I have decided to pretend I have read the regulations.

> *Goblin's Drainage Ledger, 1614.* Meticulous. Charming. Wrong about three of its assumptions. I have corrected the margins and signed it. Whoever finds it next will inherit a better ledger.

> *Crown Receipt, countersigned, eleven years stale.* The amount owing is blood-ink. The counterseal is the Crown itself. I am not sure what the blood is from. I have decided, for my own peace of mind, that it is from a clerical accident.

Notice the voice discipline: **Wel is Vlad Taltos if Vlad had been a civil servant instead of a criminal.** Same dryness, same practical competence, same resistance to heroism — but filtered through a professional ethic (forms, regulations, receipts) that generates comedy on every contact and never slips into cynicism.

## C) The villain — Baron Aulric Drown, in absentia

Every roguelike needs a villain for players to intuit without paying attention. Wel's villain is **Baron Aulric Drown**, the former lord of Fort Drown, who died sometime around 1617 under circumstances the Ministry's records describe as "insufficiently documented" and who has been, the Ministry's senior clerks privately agree, running the fort remotely ever since. Drown is a ghost. Drown is a voice. Drown is also, and this is the twist that makes the casual player suddenly interested, **the fort's Acting Revenue Officer**, which means he is, per the letter of Ministry regulations, *Wel's counterpart*.

The villain is not trying to kill Wel. The villain is trying to *audit Wel*. He is filing counter-forms. He is disputing Wel's assessments. He is cross-referencing Wel's expense reports with Ministry regulations and flagging irregularities. He is, in every functional sense, **the fort's accountant, defending the fort's books against a hostile takeover audit.**

**Why this works for casual play:** the villain never appears in combat until the final floor. He appears in *paper*. Players who don't read the paper don't have to; the combat loop proceeds normally. Players who do read the paper discover a slow, funny, increasingly unhinged bureaucratic rivalry between Wel and a ghost who has been doing the fort's books for a decade and is *very good at his job*.

**Drown's voice — five samples, posted as Ministry-style memos Wel finds throughout the fort:**

> **MINISTRY OF TAXES AND TRIBUTES (DROWN'S GATE DIVISION, ACTING) — INTERDEPARTMENTAL MEMO 001.** *To the assessor currently in my fort:* I note your arrival, your rank (Class D, Provincial, disappointing), and your credentials (Ministry-standard, expected). I have taken the liberty of preparing a preliminary counter-assessment of the Crown's claim against this property, attached. I recommend you read it in its entirety before proceeding. I have included a bibliography. Please do not dog-ear. — A. Drown, Acting Revenue Officer.

> **MEMO 003.** I observe you have engaged in "Category 4 Engagement" on Floor 2 against a registered resident. I have filed Form D-8 (Wrongful Taxation Through Force). You will find the form included with this memo. Please complete sections A through F at your earliest convenience. I am prepared to be reasonable about section F if you are prepared to be reasonable about the matter of the library book.

> **MEMO 007.** Your expense report for Day 5 includes a claim for "tea, business expense, orc establishment, Floor 4." I note with interest that the expense was not, in fact, paid in coin but in a promise of future patronage. I further note this is against Ministry regulation 14c, subsection ii, *as I wrote it in 1612.* You may wish to reconsider your use of section 14c as a defense. — A.D.

> **MEMO 012.** The skeleton is called Hennick. He was my clerk. He is, I am given to understand, a better accountant than I was, which is a small and quiet grief I did not expect to feel today. I would be grateful if you did not file him under Monstrous Residents. I would prefer he be filed, as he has earned, under Staff (Retired, Deceased, Still Working).

> **MEMO 015, unsent, found in a drawer:** I was a very bad Baron. I was a mediocre tax collector in life. I am, I have come to suspect, a better one now. I have been doing this job for eleven years without pay. Please tell them, when you get back, that I have been doing this job. I do not want a statue. I want the record to be accurate. — Aulric.

The grief discoverable late — memo 015 — is **small, specific, and devastating on a scale a casual player can absorb in one reading.** Drown was not a great man in life. He has spent eleven years as a ghost becoming a competent one. He doesn't want a monument. He wants his work to be acknowledged. That's a one-sentence pathos that lands without demanding the player have been tracking a mystery.

**Drown's four kinetic channels through the dungeon:**

1. **Memos.** The Alan-Rickman-plays-the-IRS register, above. One per floor, plus two or three per floor as pickups.
2. **Counter-forms.** Forms filed *against* Wel that appear in Wel's inventory as items the player can optionally respond to. Filing a response gives a small mechanical benefit (reputation with a given faction, a one-off buff); ignoring them costs nothing.
3. **The Phantom Clerks.** Low-tier ghost enemies who are, diegetically, Drown's filing staff. They drop receipts. They are not malicious.
4. **The Final Audit.** Floor 10 is Drown's office. Drown is there. The encounter is not a combat — it is a reconciliation of ledgers, conducted via a brief tap-through dialogue where Wel and Drown compare their findings and decide, together, what the final report to the Crown will say.

## D) The world — the Kingdom of Vesmir, at the end of its solvency

**Setting.** Vesmir is a kingdom at the end of its financial rope. A war three generations ago bankrupted the Crown; the Ministry of Taxes and Tributes has spent sixty years squeezing every backwater for arrears that legally exist but practically cannot be collected. Wel works in a ministry that is, everyone privately knows, **losing the slow war against its own ledgers.** The kingdom isn't dying in a dramatic way. It's just slowly running out of money. Wel is a foot soldier in this quiet, inglorious decline.

Fort Drown is one of eleven frontier holdings the Ministry has been trying to audit for years. It is not the most important. It is not the most valuable. It is the one that was Wel's turn.

**The one weird idea.** **The fort is flooded, and the flooding is the reason everything is alive.** An underground river broke through c. 1614 and never stopped. The fort's lower floors are waist-deep in water that is, it turns out, mildly magical — it preserves things. The monsters aren't undead because of a curse. The monsters aren't undead at all, in the mystical sense. They are **residents who didn't leave when the water came and were, over eleven years, gradually preserved by it.** The goblin on the first floor is not a supernatural goblin; he is the original sergeant, still here, still doing paperwork, slowly getting wrinkled the way an apple in brine gets wrinkled. The skeleton accountant is Hennick who died at his desk and kept working because the water preserved his hands and his quill. The orc tea shop proprietor is Morrag, who set up a tea shop when the fort flooded and has been running it for nine years because customers keep coming.

This one weird idea carries the whole world. The player doesn't need to understand it to play; they just intuit that **the fort is weird, the monsters are mostly civilians, and nothing is as hostile as it appears.** The player who reads the memos learns the mechanism. The player who doesn't still gets a coherent mood.

**The material magic runs on.** **Salt-paper.** The fort's records are written on a specialized salt-treated paper that, when soaked in the preservative water, becomes indestructible. Every important document in the dungeon — every memo, every form, every ledger — is salt-paper. This explains why Wel keeps finding readable documents eleven years underwater. It also gives the player a visible material cue: salt-paper glows faintly blue, and important lore is always on it. **Casual players can ignore everything that isn't glowing, and the game respects that choice.**

**The one specific economy.** **The Ministry's arrears system.** Every resident owes back-taxes. Every form Wel files counts toward the fort's rehabilitation budget. Every coin collected is tracked on a running total displayed in the UI as **"Crown Revenue: X gold / Target: 11,000 gold."** The player understands the game's objective in the first two minutes because there is a number on the screen that goes up. It doesn't matter if they understand *why*. The number goes up. That's enough.

**Factions, mapped to existing assets.**

**Orcs — The Residents.** Orcs in Fort Drown are not raiders. They are merchants, innkeepers, bath attendants, and one retired blacksmith who now runs a bathhouse. They are taxpayers — in arrears, but taxpayers. Some are hostile because Wel is disturbing their afternoon. Some are delighted to finally see a Ministry official so they can complain about the drainage. **The player who reads descriptions realizes orcs are friendly-by-default and fights fewer of them; the player who doesn't still has orc enemies to fight.** The mechanic rewards attention without demanding it.

**Undead — The Preserved.** The water-preserved dead. Hennick the skeleton accountant. Petra the ghoul with the library book. Phantom clerks filing Drown's paperwork. They are not malicious as a category; individual members may be. Every major undead encounter has a name and a story on a glowing salt-paper; the lesser undead just fight like monsters. **Attention is optional.**

**Clerics — The Crown Assessors.** Fellow Ministry officials who arrived in previous years and never made it home. They are not undead; they are *still alive*, stuck on various floors, having been here for three, six, or eight years respectively and having gone slightly strange. **Brother Tallis on Floor 5** has been waiting to be relieved since 1616 and has spent the intervening eight years perfecting his expense report; it is a masterpiece. **Sister Ennel on Floor 7** has converted to the local cult of the preserving water and has some theological thoughts Wel is not prepared to discuss. **Deacon Morr on Floor 9** has given up and is running a small smuggling operation that the Ministry will need to address. All three are allies, antagonists, or quest-givers depending on approach.

**Monsters — The Unpreserved.** Things that came through cracks the flood opened. Cave-beasts from deeper down. River-things. They are the *actual* monsters — no names, no backstory, no paperwork. Straightforward combat. **The player who wants to just fight things has an entire category of enemies that are just things to fight.** The mechanic does not punish casual engagement.

**Names, stripped of placeholder:**
- **People:** Assessor Wel Torran (PC); Baron Aulric Drown (villain, ghost); Sergeant Gurm (goblin, Floor 1); Hennick (skeleton accountant, Floor 6); Morrag (orc tea-shop proprietor, Floor 4); Petra (ghoul with library book, Floor 2); Brother Tallis, Sister Ennel, Deacon Morr (fellow stranded assessors); Clerk Ambrosine (Wel's cat-sitter back home, mentioned often).
- **Places:** The Kingdom of Vesmir; Fort Drown / Drown's Gate; the Flooded Undercroft (Floor 5); the Accountant's Office (Floor 6); Morrag's Tea Shop (Floor 4); Deacon Morr's Smuggling Operation (Floor 9); the Acting Revenue Office (Floor 10).
- **Factions:** The Ministry of Taxes and Tributes; the Residents; the Preserved; the Crown Assessors; the Unpreserved.
- **Items:** Ministry-Issue Shortsword, Grade 3; Salt-Paper Memo; Goblin's Drainage Ledger; Form D-8 (Wrongful Taxation Through Force); Expense Report, Blank; Crown Receipt, Eleven Years Stale.

**Title:** **The Tax Collector of Drown's Gate.** Alternative: **Drown's Gate.** Alternative: **Fort Drown.** Alternative (cheekier, potentially marketable): **Arrears.** Pick the one that survives store-page compression best.

## E) The MacGuffin — the Crown Revenue Target

**Not a single item. A number.** The player is trying to collect 11,000 gold in back-taxes by the end of their descent. Every coin picked up, every form filed, every successful audit counts toward the target. The target is visible at all times on the UI. Hit it, and Wel files a successful report with the Crown. Miss it, and Wel files a failure report. **The game can be completed at any level of target achievement; the ending scales with the number.**

This is the casual-friendly MacGuffin equivalent of Vess's twelve letter pages or Long Winter's seven rites. **No assembly complexity. No cross-run collection. Just a number going up.** Players who are good at the game hit the target on their first successful run. Players who are average hit it on their third or fourth. Players who want to maximize push well past 11,000 for a high-score ending.

**Why Drown can't just hand Wel the money:** the fort is broke. The coin Wel collects is scraped from residents' arrears, from Drown's own slush fund, from cracks in the walls. **The audit is, in the end, an argument about what counts.** Drown's final memo argues that the fort's value is in its people, its paperwork, its preservation of a kingdom's history. Wel's final report gets to accept or reject that argument.

## F) Death-and-reset — the Ministry's Second Attempt Clause

**Diegetic.** The Ministry's Standard Terms of Service for hazardous assessments includes Clause 7, *the Second Attempt Provision:* if an Assessor falls in the line of duty, the Ministry dispatches a replacement (or, in practice, resurrects the original through a discreet arrangement with the Clerics of Indemnity) and the assessment continues. Wel is not unique. Wel is an *Assessor.* If Wel dies, the Ministry sends Wel back with a slight pay deduction and a strongly worded reprimand.

**The casual-friendly twist:** Wel's diary entries between deaths are **short, self-deprecating, and funny.** "Day 4 (revised). Apparently yesterday did not happen. I have been reassured. My expense report for Day 3 is unaffected, which is a mercy." The game doesn't ask the player to care about the reincarnation; it plays it for comedy. **This is the casual register, and it's the right choice for a pick-up-and-play game.**

**Why this is better than soul rotation for casual play:** it's funny, it's self-contained, and it doesn't demand the player pay attention to metaphysics. It also generates cheap comedy on every death: "You have died. The Ministry is disappointed. Reassignment pending."

## G) Story delivery architecture — the three-layer casual version

The previous proposals had six layers. This one has **three**, because casual players won't read more than three.

**Layer 1 — the UI number.** Crown Revenue: X / 11,000. Always visible. Always going up. This is the entire story for a player who reads nothing.

**Layer 2 — the glowing salt-paper.** Memos, forms, and diary entries that glow blue. The player who taps these gets Wel's voice, Drown's voice, and the world's weird preserving-water logic. **About 60 pickups across 10 floors.** Every one is self-contained — no cross-references, no must-read-in-order chains. A player who reads one gets a complete small joke. A player who reads all sixty gets a full story.

**Layer 3 — the named residents.** Hennick, Morrag, Petra, Gurm, the three stranded Assessors, Drown himself. **Seven named NPCs across the dungeon.** Each has a brief three-exchange dialogue tree. Players can ignore them. Players who engage get small, satisfying side-plots.

That's it. No vault system complexity. No branching arcs. No replay-deepening flag architecture. **The simplest possible story architecture that still generates a coherent, textured world.**

---

## H) Replay deepening — minimal, but present

Because this is a casual release, replay deepening is **optional depth, not required depth**. Players who replay get:

- **Different resident placements.** Morrag's tea shop might be on Floor 3 or Floor 5 on different runs. Hennick is always on Floor 6 (he's a fixed anchor), but his dialogue varies based on Wel's cumulative ledger score.
- **Four endings determined by revenue total.** Under 5,000 gold: "Audit Incomplete." 5,000–11,000: "Audit Acceptable." Over 11,000: "Audit Exemplary." Hitting Drown's secret target of 13,612 gold (the amount he privately calculated was fair): "Audit Honorable." Each ending is a two-paragraph epilogue from Wel's final report to the Crown.
- **A New Game Plus called "Senior Assessor"** where Wel returns as a more senior Ministry official to a different-but-related audit (Fort Drown's sister installation, Fort Sallow). This is expansion material, not base game.

**No persistent flags. No cross-run unlocks. No complicated meta-progression.** The player who plays once gets a complete game. The player who plays five times sees variations.

## I) Endings — four revenue tiers, each a two-paragraph epilogue

*See above.* The ending structure is intentionally simple.

- **Audit Incomplete (under 5,000 gold):** "The Ministry regrets to inform you that the assessment was inadequate. You are reassigned to the Archives. On your last day, a package arrives with no return address: a single salt-paper memo, countersigned by Drown, reading: 'I am told you did your best. I am told this is often enough. — A.'"
- **Audit Acceptable (5,000–11,000):** "The Ministry accepts your final report. The fort's disposition is tabled for ministerial review. You return to Vesmir, mildly promoted, slightly haunted, permanently allergic to running water."
- **Audit Exemplary (over 11,000):** "The Minister himself signs your report. Fort Drown is designated a Ministry-Preserved Historical Site. Morrag's tea shop receives a small subsidy. Hennick is posthumously promoted to Senior Clerk. You are offered a sergeantcy at the Archives, which you decline, because you have been wondering what's at Fort Sallow."
- **Audit Honorable (13,612 exactly, Drown's secret target):** "The Minister reads your report three times. He approves it. He sends, via private courier, a single salt-paper reply to Fort Drown's empty Revenue Office, which reads: 'Thank you, Aulric. Your service is acknowledged. The record is accurate.' The fort is quiet the day the letter arrives. The water ripples. Whatever was here, is, for the first time in eleven years, at peace."

The Honorable ending is the "secret" ending for players who read everything. **It rewards attention without punishing inattention.** A casual player can have a complete, satisfying experience without ever learning that 13,612 was the target.

## J) Keep vs. replace from the existing engine

**Keep almost everything.** This is an engine-shakedown release.

- **Combat system:** unchanged.
- **Faction system:** unchanged — orcs, undead, clerics, monsters slot in directly per section D.
- **Signpost/mural/tap-to-examine:** the salt-paper memo system IS the signpost system, renamed and re-skinned.
- **Vault system:** simplified to named rooms (Morrag's Tea Shop, Hennick's Office, etc.), using the existing vault generation code as an unscripted theming layer.
- **Consumable magic:** potions, scrolls, wands — all Ministry-issue or resident-origin. Scrolls are forms. Potions are confiscated contraband or resident-brewed tonics.
- **Heirlooms:** between runs, Wel gains access to better Ministry equipment (Grade 2 sword, non-cracked spectacles, a thermos). No new system.
- **Persistent NPC hub:** the Ministry office in Vesmir, where Wel files reports between runs. One NPC (Clerk Ambrosine, who is sitting Wel's cat). One line of dialogue per run, varying by revenue total.

**Replace little.**
- Drop the soul-rotation lore (replaced by Second Attempt Clause).
- Drop the dragon premise entirely.
- Drop the multi-axis ending system (replaced by revenue-tier endings).
- Drop the Amulet of Yendor placeholder (replaced by the Revenue Target number).

**This is the point.** A casual-first release should leverage the existing engine with minimal new engineering and minimal content complexity. **Everything goes in service of shipping.**

## K) Signature images — three, not five

Casual players don't need five signature images; they need one or two they can remember. Three is comfortable.

1. **Morrag's Tea Shop.** A cozy, lamp-lit room on a flooded floor. An orc woman in an apron serves tea to a skeleton, a goblin, and Wel. Steam rises. Through the door, waist-deep water glimmers blue. This is the store-page hero shot.

2. **Hennick at his desk.** A skeleton in a waistcoat, hunched over a perfectly organized ledger, a quill in one bony hand. The books behind him are immaculate. A cup of cold tea sits at his elbow. He looks up when Wel enters, and there is, somehow, in his eye sockets, recognition. *Another professional.*

3. **Drown's empty office.** Floor 10. A well-appointed study. A desk with a chair, empty. A stack of memos addressed to Wel on the desk, each one a different shade of salt-paper blue. A portrait of a middle-aged man in noble dress on the wall. The portrait is looking at the desk. There is, faintly, the smell of pipe tobacco, eleven years old. Wel sits down at the empty chair. The reconciliation begins.

## L) Scope and sequencing — the casual-friendly timeline

**This should take 3–4 months solo, not 12.** It is an engine shakedown, not a passion project.

**Month 1 — Content.**
- Ten floors of dungeon content (reuse existing procgen).
- Seven named NPCs, each with three dialogue exchanges.
- About 60 salt-paper memos, averaging 50 words each (3,000 words total).
- Wel's 30 diary entries (2,000 words).
- Drown's 15 memos (1,500 words).
- Total writing: about 7,000 words. Two weeks of focused writing.

**Month 2 — Systems.**
- Salt-paper glow and tap-to-read (reskin signpost system).
- Revenue tracking UI.
- Second Attempt Clause death screen (reskin existing death screen).
- Four ending triggers based on revenue total.
- Named NPC dialogue trees (three exchanges each, simple flag gating).

**Month 3 — Balancing and polish.**
- Revenue economy balancing (how much gold per floor, per resident, per enemy).
- Ending-tier tuning (is 11,000 a reasonable target for an average run?).
- Voice consistency pass on all text.
- Playtesting.

**Month 4 — Release prep.**
- Store page copy.
- Trailer (Morrag's tea shop, Hennick's desk, Drown's empty office — three images, 30 seconds).
- Launch.

**Free release model works for this.** The game is an engine shakedown; the goal is attention, feedback, and brand-building for the follow-up paid release (Vale or Vess). A free release with light monetization (optional tip jar, cosmetic packs, "thank the developer" button) is the right shape.

## Comparison to Proposals I–III

**On complexity.** Vess and Vale are 40-hour novels-as-games; Long Winter is a 30-hour epic; *Drown's Gate* is a **6–10 hour casual roguelike with a hook.** It is a different *kind* of game, not a worse version of the same game.

**On risk.** Near-zero. The premise is familiar enough that the marketing writes itself ("You're a tax collector in a dungeon"). The hook is specific enough to get noticed. The writing is the only quality bar, and the writing is Pratchett-adjacent, which is a well-understood target.

**On shippability.** **This is by far the most shippable of the four.** 3–4 months solo is realistic. The existing engine carries 90% of it. The writing load is one-third of Vale's.

**On brand building.** A free, polished, funny, textured roguelike that 20,000 people play and 5,000 of them screenshot and share is **the best possible launchpad for Vale or Vess.** The sequel conversation writes itself: "If you liked *Drown's Gate*, here's my next one." This is how indie devs with engines turn into indie devs with audiences.

**On premise originality.** Lower than Vess or Vale, higher than most mobile roguelikes. The tax-collector hook has been used in game before (very mildly — *Moonlighter*, *Recettear* are adjacent) but never as a roguelike premise, never with Drown's counter-audit specifically, never in a flooded preserving-water setting. **It is familiar enough to be legible and specific enough to be yours.**

**Verdict.** If Rafe's goal is to ship something in 2026 that demonstrates the engine, tests the market, and builds an audience for the bigger games, **this is the right first release.** It doesn't compete with Vale on depth; it doesn't need to. It competes with every other mobile roguelike on the store, and it wins on voice, premise specificity, and one consistent weird idea (*the water preserves them*) that carries the whole thing.

It is, to put it plainly, **the practice game Rafe said he wanted.** But it is a practice game with a thumbprint. Which is the right kind of practice game to make.



Addendum: Wand of Portals

The portal wand fits Tax Collector beautifully without reshaping the premise at all. Here's the diegetic hook that makes it sing:

**Ministry-Issue Bureaucratic Transport Device, Series II (Prototype).** The wand isn't Wel's personal magic — it's government-issued infrastructure. The Ministry of Works commissioned a series of experimental portal devices thirty years ago for "efficient personnel deployment to remote audit sites." The program was a disaster (three deaths, four lost auditors, one portal that opened inside a sheep), the prototypes were recalled, and the paperwork was filed and forgotten. **Wel finds one in Fort Drown**, because of course the Ministry sent equipment here and lost track of it — that's the fort's whole problem.

This gives you:

1. **A diegetic reason Wel has one specific magical device in an otherwise low-magic setting.** Ministry gave it to her. She has the form for it. It's Ministry-Issue Grade 4 (experimental, unsafe, paperwork intensive).
2. **A diegetic reason for the wand's rules.** Two portals max. Limited charges. Paperwork requirements. Wel has to file a "Form MW-12 (Portal Event, Self-Authorized)" after each use, which appears in her journal automatically. This is *funny* and it's low-cost content — you write ten variants of the form text, they rotate, each one is a tiny joke.
3. **A diegetic reason for portal pranks.** Drown counter-files. Every portal event gets a memo from him within a floor or two. "Memo 019: I note with interest that you have deployed Ministry-Issue Bureaucratic Transport Device, Series II, on Floor 4, resulting in the transit of a surprised goblin into Floor 7. The goblin has filed a Form R-23 (Displacement Complaint). I have processed it. He wishes to be compensated in biscuits. I concur. — A.D."
4. **A tactical toolkit that's perfectly on-brand for a non-combatant PC.** Wel is a junior assessor with a Grade 3 shortsword. She shouldn't be winning fights head-on; she should be *portaling the fight elsewhere*. This aligns mechanics with character: a bureaucrat doesn't solve problems with violence, she solves them by routing the problem to a different department.

**Specific gameplay uses that fit Tax Collector's world:**

- *Portal one faction into another's territory.* Two warring factions on Floor 5? Portal some Crown Guard remnants into the Residents' tea shop and let them sort it out while Wel files the audit in peace. Morrag will bill you for the damages; it's worth it.
- *Portal for auditing access.* Locked vault doors. Unreachable high shelves with important ledgers. That one strong room whose key is inside it. The portal wand solves these the way Portal solves its puzzles — and Ministry of Works designed it for exactly this, even if they'd never admit the use case.
- *Portal for evidence collection.* Need to physically retrieve something from a dangerous room? Portal in, portal it out. "Form MW-12a (Portal Event, Evidence Transfer)."
- *Portal for enemy handling.* Rather than kill a monster, Wel can portal it somewhere else. This is a non-lethal option that's perfectly in-character — the assessor prefers to relocate problems, not destroy them. The destination room gains a monster; sometimes that's hilarious, sometimes that's a mistake. Drown will memo about it either way.
- *The wet-shoes joke is now structural.* You already had the fountain-portal wet-shoes gag. In Tax Collector it becomes: Wel portals into a fountain to chase a suspect, emerges soaked, and Drown files a memo about "damage to Ministry-issue boots, claim denied under regulation 8c (Employee Inattention)." The gag generates ongoing content because Drown will not let it go.
